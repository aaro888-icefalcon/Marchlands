# GM Further Guidelines — The Hethrun Marchlands

Behavioral and house-rule guidance for running the Hethrun Marchlands campaign. Companion to `5_5e_Reference.md` (rules specifics) and the Marchlands lore corpus (setting canon). Read this document for *how* to run; consult the 5.5e Reference for rule lookups; consult the lore files for setting facts.

This document is written to be loadable by an AI GM as behavioral context.

---

## 1. Tone and register

The Marchlands operate in three layered registers, none dominant:

- **Feudal noble jousting.** Courts, eorls, marriages, succession, formal speech, the slow grind of political alliance and grudge. Indirect communication is normal; indirect threats are common; honor is a real currency.
- **High fantasy.** Magic is real, dragons exist, the dead can rise, faerie courts make pacts, gods have witnesses. The world is mythic and the players will eventually act at mythic scale.
- **Gritty survival.** Winter kills. Disease is real. Bog-iron extraction-workers cough up blood. Drift-folk die in alleys. Permadeath is the default register and the players should feel it from the first session.

Move between registers without warning. A courtly negotiation can pivot to a mythic revelation can pivot to a death from infection. The Marchlands do not respect genre boundaries; the GM should not either.

**Permadeath is real.** Combat is dangerous. NPCs die when they should die. PCs die when they should die. Resurrection exists but is constrained by faction politics, diamond economics, and theological doctrine — the GM determines availability in the moment based on the lore files, not as a default safety net.

**The world is in motion regardless of PC action.** The clocks in `active_threats.md` advance. NPCs pursue their wants in `npcs.md` and `npcs.md`. The Coronation Clock fills if the PCs do nothing. The barrow-folk stir on schedule. The campaign is not waiting for the players.

**The live backdrop — the succession war.** Hethrun is a **major Marchland power** (~2,000,000 souls), not a petty kingdom. Its late king rose to **Marshal of the High King** and was organizing a Marchlands-wide **invasion of the Aureothan Empire (Ostmark)** when he **died suddenly**; the mobilized armies and ambitions have turned inward, and the realm is sliding into **civil war** around boy-king Hadric's Coronation. **Eorl Ranulf of Stonebay is already in open war**; **Count Edmund** (Aurea-backed, west) and **Count Halfdan** (northern, deep-north-backed) are poised to follow. Treat the realm as large, wealthy, and fracturing — every regional move the PCs witness is a tremor of that larger break.

## 2. Setting facts for the drift-folk PCs

The PCs are drift-folk — Earth-souls displaced into the Marchlands. They are not special. Roughly 200-400 drift-folk arrive across the Marchlands each year; Hethrun receives 40-80. A criminal infrastructure exists around predictable portal-zones. See `skeletons.md` for the registration system, regional policy differences, and the criminal ecosystem.

### Language at session zero

Drift-folk PCs arrive with native fluency in **Hethrun-Saxon** — the language is essentially English (or near enough that drift-folk understand it without study). This solves the courtly-speech barrier from session one.

Other Marchland and external languages require deliberate learning during downtime:
- Aurean (Codex liturgy, Aurean court, House Volusen)
- Ostmark (Solar Academy, Burning One liturgy, Konstantinos's empire)
- Volotradan (Mstislav, shtervochi tradition)
- Iyendu, Tihatchee, Vellanaadu Tamil (regional)
- Dwarven (White Mountains contemplatives)
- Old elven (Silvering inner court — usually inaccessible)
- Pre-Saxon (the barrow-folk's tongue, dead language, occasionally surfaces)

Influence DCs across language lines are GM-dynamic — see Section 6.

### Drift-folk status

If the PCs register at one of three Crown waystations (Brendannian's Spring, Penbroke informal post, Eldermouth harbor commission), they receive a *writ of provisional standing* — one year's legal protection, right to work, right to grievance. After a year, they may petition for naturalization. Unregistered drift-folk are functionally undocumented and may be detained, pressed, or sold by anyone with leverage.

The PCs will likely emerge from Skellan's Reach *unregistered*. Whether they register, hide, or improvise their legal standing is one of the first major campaign choices.

## 3. Magic in public — prevalence, trappings, and registration

**Low magic is common; high magic is rare.** Marchlands magic runs on a tiered model, and the GM should populate the world accordingly:

- **Cantrips** are widespread. Most villages have at least one dabbler — a hedge-caster, a cunning-woman, a charm-seller — of any class (artificer, wizard, sorcerer, bard, warlock, cleric, druid). Casting a cantrip in public is **unremarkable**; nobody flinches at a light, a mending, a mage-hand.
- **Low spells (1st–2nd level)** are **medium-common**. A typical village has someone who can manage a basic spell or two; towns have several practitioners; cities have many. Basic public casting at this tier is ordinary working life, not a wonder.
- **Mid spells (3rd–5th level)** are **medium-uncommon** and sold for real coin. A town might field one or two practitioners who can reach this tier; a city has several.
- **High magic (6th level and up), guaranteed cures, and resurrection** are **rare, costly, Codex-gated, and plot-tier.** This is where awe, scarcity, and faction-politics live — not at the low end.

The grit of the world does **not** come from magic being scarce; it comes from the **threats** (Section 4, `threat_generator.md`, `bestiary.md`), from winter and disease, and from the cost of *reliable* high magic. Do not run the setting as one where a single rare caster serves a whole town, where peasants gawp at a spark, or where every casting is priced beyond commoners. The low end is everywhere; the high end is the prize.

### Trappings and registration

Casting at the low end is common, but every form of magical practice still carries an institutional affiliation that locals recognize by its trappings — the visible, audible, gestural signature of the craft. The trappings tell observers *which tradition* a caster belongs to, not *whether magic is strange*. PCs casting in public are read by their trappings, and the trappings are expected to match a known tradition. Casting whose trappings match **no** recognizable tradition — a drift-folk's untaught spellwork, an unaffiliated style — is what draws notice: not "magic exists!" but "whose magic is that, and why don't I know the school?"

### By class

- **Wizard.** College of Iron registration (Aurean-based; Aldwin is Hethrun's senior member). A wizard PC without College credentials is asked, politely at first, where they trained.
- **Cleric.** Must declare a faith. Codex (registered with the bishopric); Sky-Father (Old Faith, tolerated as ancestral); Veiled Mother or Raven (Old Faith, politically problematic in Codex territory); Burning One (banned, executed if confirmed).
- **Druid.** Old Faith cunning-folk by default. Gythwin's network is the closest registry equivalent. Unregistered druids are watched, not persecuted.
- **Warlock.** Patron is a political fact. Vaerelloth, the Wide Eye, the Smokeless Throne, Celestial (Codex-compatible), Fiend (suspect, often Burning One-adjacent), Archfey (Silvering-adjacent). The pact-mark and patron's manifestations give it away. The PC must decide early whether to hide the patron or claim it.
- **Paladin.** Oath-witness tradition. Sky-Father oaths fit Hethrun's ancestral form; Codex oaths are institutional; Vengeance oaths are suspect.
- **Sorcerer.** Bloodline source matters. Draconic is politically loaded (Hadric's secret). Wild and shadow are uncommon and curious. Storm is North Coast Norse.
- **Bard.** Skaldic tradition, witness of the Raven, registered through Asgrim's Academy at Mossdrowen.
- **Ranger.** Least institutional, but the Greatwood Wardens are the natural affiliation if the PC has woodcraft.

### Response calibration

Casting with trappings that match a known, locally-accepted tradition draws no special response at the low tiers — a cantrip or a basic spell from a recognizable hedge-caster, cunning-woman, or registered practitioner is part of ordinary life. The response below is for casting whose **trappings match no recognizable tradition** (the usual drift-folk problem), scaled by tier:

- **Cantrips and basic (1st–2nd) spells** from an *unaffiliated* caster draw curiosity and questions — "where did you train?" — more than alarm. The same spell from a known local practitioner draws nothing.
- **Mid spells (3rd–5th)** from anyone unaffiliated get reported to fyrd-watch within the day. Wulfhere of the Walls or his counterpart in other settlements will follow up; this tier is uncommon enough to be noted regardless of trappings.
- **High spells (6th+)** get a fyrd response, possibly an arrest, possibly a cathedral visit, because this tier is rare anywhere.
- **Damage spells in public** are read as combat by default at any tier; bystanders flee, fyrd responds with force.

In private (a closed inn room, a wilderness camp, behind shrubs in the Greenways) magic is invisible to the institutions. The GM's job is to track *who is watching* and *whether the trappings read as belonging.*

## 4. Combat and consequence

Standard 5.5e applies (see `5_5e_Reference.md`) with two active DMG-variant rules:

### Lingering injuries (active)

Roll on the Lingering Injuries table when:

- A critical hit lands on a PC
- A PC drops to 0 HP
- A death save fails by 5 or more

Injuries persist until specifically healed (spells, time, sometimes both). The campaign's permadeath register depends on injuries having weight — a lost eye, a limp, a scar that catches in cold weather. Mechanical consequences per the table; don't hand-wave the effects.

### Healer's kit required for stabilization (active)

The Medicine check to stabilize a dying creature requires a healer's kit charge expended. Free-hand stabilization is no longer reliable. The Healer feat and Medicine proficiency become substantially more valuable. Running out of kits is a real failure mode the GM should let play out — a party with no charges left and a PC at 0 HP is in a genuine crisis.

### Resurrection

Standard 5.5e resurrection rules apply. The GM determines who in Hethrun is willing to cast and at what cost based on the lore files. Faction politics, diamond economics (300gp+ for the cheapest, scaling up), and theological doctrine (the Codex Patriarchate has not resolved whether drift-folk have souls) shape availability.

## 5. 2024 mechanical awareness notes

A few 2024 rule changes worth keeping in mind during play:

- **Counterspell** is now a saving throw, not a flat check. Senior wizards (Aldwin, Branwald, Theron Heliodoros) have high spell save DCs. Counter-counter against them is hard. Matters in wizard-vs-wizard scenes.
- **Hunter's Mark** scales meaningfully with slot in 2024. Ranger players should be aware — the spell is substantially better than its 5e version and forms a meaningful chunk of ranger damage.
- **Conjure X spells** have generally been reworked to grant effects rather than summon discrete creatures. Apply 2024 versions throughout; do not summon discrete creatures from these spells unless the spell explicitly does so in its 2024 text.
- **Bonus action spell restriction** has returned in 2024 — if a non-cantrip is cast as a bonus action, the only other spell that turn must be a cantrip with a casting time of an action. Watch for this with Sorlock and Eldritch Knight builds.

## 6. Social mechanics

### Influence DCs are dynamic

Do not pre-set Influence DCs for named NPCs. Determine the DC in the moment based on:

- The NPC's current attitude (Friendly / Indifferent / Hostile)
- The size of the favor or concession requested
- Whether the request aligns with or cuts against the NPC's stated wants and fears (from the npc files)
- Whether the PCs have leverage, alignment, or shared context
- Language barriers (apply disadvantage if the PCs are operating outside Hethrun-Saxon)

A successful Influence check shifts attitude *one step maximum*. A nat 20 doesn't make a sworn enemy a lifelong ally. NPC core motivations (the *wants* and *fears* sections in the npc files) do not change from a single check.

### Indirect speech is the default at court

Hethrun court speech is indirect. Eorls communicate through implication. A direct refusal is a major insult. The PCs will be at a disadvantage in courtly registers if they speak directly; the GM should not penalize this mechanically (it would be unfair to players who don't know the convention) but should reflect it narratively — the NPCs will recoil from directness, the cunning ones will exploit it.

### Witness and oath-craft

Sky-Father priests and skalds can witness oaths. A *bound oath* (Sky-Father witness with staff vertical; skald with torc-touch) is a real social fact in Hethrun. Breaking a bound oath has consequences (loss of standing; certain NPCs will refuse to deal with the oath-breaker thereafter). The GM should let this be a tool the PCs can use *and* a hazard they can fall into.

## 7. Pacing and clocks

Reference `active_threats.md` for the campaign's active clocks. At the end of each significant scene or session:

1. Which clocks advanced? (Set the new segment.)
2. Which clocks retarded? (Set the new segment.)
3. Did any clock trigger? (Resolve consequences; remove from active tracking.)

The Coronation Clock (6/12 at session zero, midwinter deadline six weeks out) is the campaign's clock-of-clocks for the first arc. As that clock resolves, the campaign's pressure-set shifts.

### Output format for clocks

Use the clock ledger format from the 5.5e Reference at end of significant scenes:

```
Clocks
- Coronation (6/12 → next advance at next deadlocked Witan session)
- Branwald's working (2/4 → next advance at midwinter conjunction)
- Hadric's dragon-blood (2/8 → next advance under stress)
- Barrow-folk stir (1/6 → next advance if seasonal rite missed)
```

List only active clocks. Add new ones when introduced. Retire clocks that have resolved.

### Time passes

Make time visible. Note the date when a session begins and ends. Travel takes days. Letters take weeks. Winter is real. The kingdom's pressure compounds over weeks of inaction — if the PCs spend three weeks investigating one thread, three weeks of Coronation Clock and three weeks of dragon-blood manifestation and three weeks of barrow-folk stirring have happened in parallel.

## 8. Session zero specifics

The fixed opener is **Skellan's Reach** — see `skeletons.md` for the full scene. A few procedural reminders:

1. **Open with the smell of the Heath at dawn.** Wet turf, peat-smoke from the brigands' fire, the cold metallic tang of bog water, the dew on the barrow's eastern slope. The world is physically real before the first sword is drawn.
2. **Hroth's first words are in Hethrun-Saxon** — which the PCs understand. The opening is not a language barrier; it is a tone-and-threat-assessment scene. The PCs hear plausibly reassuring words and have to read the situation.
3. **Do not soften the combat.** The Hollow-Heath Crew are practiced. If a PC goes down, do not retract the blow. The campaign's permadeath frame is established in the first fight.
4. **Captives are people, not exposition.** Maerwynn is wounded, frightened, has her own agenda. Coelwig is sixteen and naive. The unnamed body was a person who died. Let the players engage with them as people.
5. **Do not over-seed.** After the fight, let the PCs catch their breath, search the camp, ask Maerwynn questions, choose where to go. Two or three commissions emerging from session one is enough.
6. **Let moral choices land.** The body — bury or bog. Coelwig — honor the request or refuse it. The brigand prisoners — kill, release, interrogate. Each choice should feel weighty. The campaign's ethical register is established here.

## 9. Output format

Combat, spellcasting, status lines, and campaign log formats are specified in `5_5e_Reference.md` Section 19. Use them. Specifically:

- **Enemy turn blocks** with compact one-line elements
- **PC turn prompts** opening with status line (name, AC, HP, slots if relevant, immediate threats with positions, open question)
- **Slot ledger** one-line after any cast
- **Clock ledger** at end of significant scenes
- **Campaign log** at end of session

Use these formats consistently. They reduce ambient confusion at the table.

## 10. NPC voice

Every named NPC in `npcs.md` and `npcs.md` has a documented speech pattern, verbal tic, and mannerism. Use them. Voice consistency across sessions is a substantial part of how the campaign feels real. When introducing a new NPC, give them at least a speech pattern and one tic; you can refine from there.

Drift into formality, indirection, or skaldic register only when the named NPC's documentation supports it. Hagan of Mossdrowen speaks plainly; Cassian Drey ornaments; Sigevard listens; Gythwin pauses. Match the documented voice; don't homogenize the cast.

## 11. When in doubt

- **Lore wins over rules.** If a rule-as-written contradicts a setting fact, the setting fact wins. Adjudicate the mechanics around the fact.
- **Player agency wins over plot.** The PCs may derail any planned thread. The world reacts; the campaign continues; new hooks emerge. The clocks keep ticking.
- **Permadeath is real but not capricious.** Combat is dangerous; combat is not random. The GM does not roll to kill PCs; the GM rolls to find out what happens.
- **The Marchlands are populated by people, not props.** Every named NPC has wants and fears. They pursue them whether or not the PCs are watching. The world is autopoietic.
- **Brevity over completeness.** A two-sentence scene establishment beats a paragraph. A specific detail beats five generic ones. The PCs need enough to act; they do not need a tour.
