# Eorldom: the Wealdvale

The eorldom of the Wealdvale — the southern Greenways grain-vale, held by Eorl Osgar, the realm's breadbasket. This file covers the Wealdvale as a place: the market-town seat of Wealdham, Osgar's crowded court, the five thane houses, the grain-vale lands, and the local hooks. The realm frame and the marriage/feud web are in `realm_titles.md`; the Hollowford March that troubles the Wealdvale is in `independent_thanedoms.md`.

## The Wealdvale eorldom — essence

The Wealdvale is the southern Greenways — Hethrun's breadbasket, the broad grain-vale whose harvests feed the realm, held by the most reluctant eorl in Hethrun. It is rich, populous, settled, and comfortable, the opposite of the hard frontiers north and west: five thane houses hold its thanelands, more than any other eorldom. Its prosperity is also its danger. A realm sliding toward succession-war is a realm that will fight over grain, and the Wealdvale sits fat and lightly defended in the middle of that calculation. And inside the southern Greenways, like a thorn in the eorldom's own flesh, lies the Hollowford March — the independent county of Count Edmund, whose land-enclosures are displacing peasants into the Wealdvale and whose Aurean correspondence makes him the one thing that can rouse Eorl Osgar to act at all.

## Wealdham — the seat

Wealdham is a market-town of roughly 5,000 in the heart of the grain-vale — Eorl Osgar's seat, an open, prosperous town of granaries, mills, and a great market square, with no wall worth the name because for two centuries it has not needed one. It is the inland grain-market of the southern Greenways, and the realm's bread is weighed here. Two pressures define it.

**Trade Hub.** Wealdham is where the Wealdvale's harvest is gathered, stored, priced, and sent north. Its granaries are the largest in Hethrun; its mills run year-round; its market draws grain-factors from Eldermouth, drovers from the Fells, and Aurean traders up the southern road. The town is genuinely prosperous and the prosperity reaches down — Wealdham's commoners are fed, housed, and unbothered, and have been for longer than anyone living can remember.

**Quarrelsome.** Prosperity has made Wealdham fractious. Five proud thane houses, a thick layer of wealthy grain-factor families, and no clear pecking-order among any of them mean the town is a permanent tangle of disputes — over market-rights, mill-shares, granary-tolls, precedence at the eorl's table. Wealdham quarrels the way comfortable places quarrel: endlessly, pettily, and without ever quite grasping that the realm beyond the vale has sharper quarrels in store for it.

## Wealdham — quarters and notable places

Osgar's Hall stands on the rise above the market — a broad, low, comfortable hall, richly furnished and undefended, more manor than fortress. The Grain-Market is the town's heart, a vast square ringed by the stone granaries that are Wealdham's real treasury and the mill-leats that power its trade. The Factors' Row is the street of the wealthy grain-merchant houses, glass-windowed and prosperous. The Codex church of Wealdham is large, well-endowed, and comfortably uncontroversial — the Wealdvale's faith is as settled as its harvests. The Drovers' Field outside the town is where the Fell herdsmen bring their beasts down to market, a yearly meeting of two very different Hethruns.

## Wealdham — significant locals

Full NPC detail is in `npcs.md`. Resident in the eorldom: Eorl Osgar, the reluctant eorl — a comfortable, shrewd-enough, conflict-averse man who wants nothing from the Coronation crisis except to be left out of it, and who is roused to real attention by exactly one subject. Seeded local figures: Reeve Aldgyth, the Wealdham market-warden, the capable woman who actually runs the grain-trade and the granary-tolls; Hunred the Elder, an informal spokesman for the displaced Greenways peasants now crowding the southern vale, who has been trying for a year to get the eorl's court to hear them; and the married shrine-keepers of the Penbroke Crossroads — the cunning-woman Wennild and the Sky-Father priest Brother Eadric — who tend one of the few shrines in Hethrun the two faiths share, on the road at the Wealdvale's northern edge.

## Eorl Osgar's court

Osgar's Hall runs on two faults.

**Feuding Lineages.** Five thane houses sit the Wealdvale's hall-moot, and no three of them agree. The eorldom is rich enough that its houses have never had to cooperate to survive, and proud enough that each reckons itself the vale's natural first house. Osgar's hall-moot is a standing quarrel — over precedence, over grain-policy, and now over what to do about the Hollowford March — and Osgar spends most of his rule simply keeping the five from open rupture.

**Reluctant Ruler.** Osgar does not want the throne-game. He inherited a fat, peaceful eorldom and his whole ambition is to keep it fat and peaceful; the Coronation crisis is, to him, a lowland quarrel that can only cost the Wealdvale and never profit it. He has avoided committing his Witan vote not from cunning but from a deep, comfortable wish that the whole thing would resolve without him. The one matter that breaks through his reluctance is Count Edmund — because Edmund's enclosures are pushing ruined peasants into the Wealdvale by the hundred, and that, Osgar cannot ignore. The household: Osgar; his eorlswife, Lady Wyngifu, sharper and more political than her husband and quietly frustrated by his drift; and his heir, a grown son content, like his father, to let the vale run itself.

## House Wenhill — Wealdvale thane

**Seat & banner-mark:** Wenhill, a strong hall on a vale-ridge near Wealdham; crossed sickles on green.
**Head:** Thane Beorn of Wenhill — a broad, forceful, impatient man of forty-five who treats every problem as a thing to be met head-on and is contemptuous of his eorl's drift.
**Character — Spoiling for War.** Wenhill is the senior martial house of the Wealdvale and the loudest voice in the hall-moot, and Beorn has decided the Hollowford March must be dealt with by force. He wants Osgar to raise the vale's fyrd, march on Count Edmund, break the enclosures, and settle the question — and he is increasingly willing to act without his reluctant eorl's leave.
**Wants:** the Hollowford March crushed; the enclosures broken; an eorl with spine, or failing that, a free hand.
**Secret:** Beorn has begun quietly sounding which of the other four houses would follow Wenhill into a march on Edmund even over Osgar's objection — a step short of defying his eorl outright, but only a step.
**Coronation lean:** regency — specifically a *strong* regency that will discipline Count Edmund; really, Beorn wants whoever will let him have his war.
**Ties:** the natural rival of House Faldon, whose entanglement with Edmund Beorn half-suspects; pushes the displaced-peasants question that House Millbrook is drowning in.
**Hook:** Beorn will back, fund, arm, or join any move against the Hollowford March, including reckless ones; a party that wants to confront Edmund has an eager and dangerous patron in Wenhill.

## House Millbrook — Wealdvale thane

**Seat & banner-mark:** Millbrook, a hall among the mill-leats of the southern vale; a green millwheel.
**Head:** Thane Leofgyth of Millbrook — a worn, decent, overstretched woman of fifty who has not turned a hungry family from her gate in two years and is paying for it.
**Character — Strained Charity.** Millbrook's lands lie along the southern road, and the southern road is the road the ruined peasants of Count Edmund's enclosures walk. Leofgyth has taken them in — let them squat the commons, fed them through two winters, found work for those she could — and the charity is breaking the house: Millbrook's stores are thin, its own tenants resentful, its means nearly spent.
**Wants:** the enclosures stopped at the source; help carrying the refugees; the eorl's court, at last, to act.
**Secret:** there is little hidden here — Millbrook's trouble is plain. What Leofgyth has not made public is how close the house is to ruin, because to admit it is to admit the charity must end.
**Coronation lean:** regency — Leofgyth wants Edmund checked by lawful authority, and wants it before the Wealdvale's prosperity is worn through.
**Ties:** bears the human cost of the Hollowford March; an ally of House Wellow, the other house that has tried to do right by the displaced.
**Hook:** Millbrook is the humane door into the enclosure crisis — a decent house drowning in other men's refugees, whose squatters are themselves a clue-trail back to the precise pattern of Count Edmund's land-grab.

## House Faldon — Wealdvale thane

**Seat & banner-mark:** Faldon, a hall on the Wealdvale's southern border, near the Hollowford March; a red fox.
**Head:** Thane Cenric of Faldon — a smooth, agreeable, profitable man of forty-eight, always reasonable in the hall-moot and always, somehow, on the comfortable side of every question.
**Character — Quiet Defection.** Faldon has thrown in with Count Edmund. Cenric has been buying Edmund's enclosed land cheap, has married a daughter into the Hollowford March, and profits steadily from the very enclosures that are ruining the southern vale. Faldon is, in plain fact, Count Edmund's foothold inside the Wealdvale eorldom — a thane of Osgar's hall-moot who serves the March's interests within it.
**Wants:** the enclosures to continue; the profit to keep flowing; Osgar's court kept too divided to act against Edmund.
**Secret:** the whole arrangement. Cenric's reasonable, moderating voice in the hall-moot — always counselling against rash action toward the Hollowford March — is Edmund's voice. Exposure would mark Faldon a traitor to its own eorldom.
**Coronation lean:** publicly loyal to Osgar; really aligned with wherever Count Edmund leans — and Edmund, corresponding with House Volusen, leans bought-regency.
**Ties:** Count Edmund of the Hollowford March is Faldon's true principal (see `independent_thanedoms.md`); the natural enemy of House Wenhill.
**Hook:** uncovering Faldon's entanglement exposes the Hollowford March's reach into the heart of the Wealdvale — a coup for House Wenhill, for Osgar, or for anyone who wants leverage in the southern Greenways.

## House Wellow — Wealdvale thane

**Seat & banner-mark:** Wellow, an old hall in the central vale; three green leaves on white.
**Head:** Thane Godric of Wellow — a solid, plainspoken, genuinely honest man of fifty-five who says what he means, keeps what he swears, and is widely trusted because he has earned it.
**Character — Plain Loyalty.** In an eorldom of quarrelling, hedging, and quiet defection, Wellow is simply what it appears to be: Osgar's truest house, loyal without calculation, decent without secret. Godric has no faction, no grievance, no hidden suit. He keeps the eorl's peace because he believes in it and runs his lands well because that is a thane's work.
**Wants:** the Wealdvale kept whole and at peace; the displaced peasants treated decently; his eorl to do the right thing, which Godric will plainly tell him is to check Count Edmund.
**Secret:** none worth the name — which, in this corpus, is itself worth noting. Wellow's only vulnerability is that an honest house can be imposed upon by dishonest ones.
**Coronation lean:** follows Osgar — soft regency, lawful order, no enthusiasm.
**Ties:** an ally of House Millbrook in the care of the displaced; trusted by every other house, which makes Godric a natural mediator.
**Hook:** Wellow is the Wealdvale's safe ally and a dependable base — a patron with no agenda, useful precisely for that, and a steady contrast that makes the other four houses' twists land harder.

## House Norwood — Wealdvale thane

**Seat & banner-mark:** Norwood, a smaller hall at the wooded western edge of the vale; a brown owl.
**Head:** old Thane Eadwulf of Norwood — frail, failing, eighty, and dying by inches; the question is no longer whether he goes but who follows him.
**Character — Open Succession.** Norwood's head is dying with the succession unsettled. Two heirs stand ready — a capable, well-liked natural son whom Eadwulf never wed the mother of, and a legitimate but weak and ill-tempered nephew with the better claim in strict law — and the Norse custom that lets a hall-moot set aside an unfit heir means either could plausibly inherit. The house, its lands, its hall-moot vote, and a slice of the Wealdvale's balance all hang on which.
**Wants:** Eadwulf wants the house to survive him; each heir wants the seat; the neighbouring houses each want the heir who suits them.
**Secret:** the contest is already being meddled with — Wenhill favours the capable son who would back a march on Edmund, Faldon quietly favours the weak nephew who could be steered, and the realm-factions have begun to notice that a Wealdvale vote is about to come loose.
**Coronation lean:** uncommitted and frozen until the succession resolves; the heir who wins decides which way Norwood leans.
**Ties:** a contained mirror of the realm's larger succession quarrels — and a vote the bigger houses are racing to shape.
**Hook:** a self-contained Wealdvale intrigue — a party can back an heir, broker between them, or exploit the contest; whoever inherits Norwood shifts the eorldom's internal balance and Osgar's hall-moot arithmetic.

## Minor settlements of the eorldom

Seeded for texture; each is a name, community tags, and a hook.

**Sheafton** — village, *Honest Folk · Trade Hub.* A large prosperous grain-village on the southern road, all granaries and threshing-floors, where the Wealdvale's harvest is first gathered before it goes up to Wealdham; decent folk, and a good place to hear what the vale is really saying.

**Coldharbour** — village, *Oppressed · Crowded Hearth.* A southern-vale village on House Millbrook's land, swollen past its means with squatting refugees from the enclosures; tense, hungry, and the clearest window in the Wealdvale onto what Count Edmund's March is doing.

**Edgeley** — hamlet, *Sealed Lips · Tradition-Bound.* A small hamlet at the wooded western edge near the Old Forest, its folk quiet and old-fashioned, keeping the boundary-rites and warning travellers — those they warn at all — away from the forbidden trees.

## Minor courts and lesser halls

**The Wealdham granary-wardens** — court tag *Caretaker Regime.* Reeve Aldgyth and the market-wardens who keep the granaries, set the tolls, and manage the grain-trade are a quiet court of real power — in a famine or a war, whoever commands the Wealdham granaries commands the realm's bread.

**The Penbroke Crossroads Shrine** — court tag *Shared Authority.* The road-junction shrine at the Wealdvale's northern edge, where the cunning-woman Wennild and the Sky-Father priest Brother Eadric — married, and of two faiths — keep one of the rare places the Old Faith and the Sky-Father priesthood cooperate. A party that needs the faiths to work together starts here. (The shrine sits on the road in from the crown demesne; see also `eorldom_eldermouth.md`.)

## The eorldom's lands — the southern Greenways

The Wealdvale's lands are the broad arable river-valleys of the southern Greenways — the kingdom's richest farmland, descending toward the Greatwood and the southern road to Aurea. It is settled, gentle country, thick with villages and freemen farms, and it rarely needs a wilderness roll. Its features are fewer than the frontier eorldoms' and quieter:

**The grain-vale** itself — leagues of cornland, mill-leats, and granary-villages along the southern road, the connective ground of the eorldom and the source of all its wealth and weight.

**The Old Forest** — at the Wealdvale's southwestern edge, between the southern Greenways and the Greatwood verge, stands forbidden ground. Once an Old Faith sacred site, the prohibition was re-affirmed by Codex authority eight decades ago when a heresy-cult was rooted out of it; the ban has stayed. Trespassers face Codex penalty and Old Faith retaliation at once. The forest holds a functioning Old Faith shrine, the heresy-cult's old ritual site, and a third thing neither priesthood will discuss. *Layered-mystery site; going there is a political risk, not only a wilderness one.*

**The Penbroke Crossroads Shrine** — the shared Sky-Father / Veiled Mother shrine at the road-junction on the Wealdvale's northern border, kept by Wennild and Brother Eadric. *Visit-site; the realm's clearest example of the two faiths at peace.* (Bestiary: the Road-Outlaws, `bestiary.md` Group 4A, work the southern road; displaced refugees are a frequent non-combat encounter.)

## Adventure hooks

- **The Hollowford March** (tier 1–2, the eorldom's spine) — Count Edmund's enclosures are ruining peasants and crowding the Wealdvale, and the eorldom's whole politics turn on what to do about him; the March itself is detailed in `independent_thanedoms.md`, and Osgar's Witan vote is, in effect, for sale to whoever checks Edmund.
- **The refugee crisis** (tier 1) — House Millbrook is breaking under the displaced; a party can help carry the burden, and the refugees' own accounts map Edmund's enclosure pattern.
- **Faldon's defection** (tier 1–2) — uncovering that Thane Cenric of Faldon serves Count Edmund inside Osgar's own hall-moot is a genuine coup with several eager buyers.
- **The Norwood succession** (tier 1) — old Eadwulf is dying with two heirs contesting; a contained intrigue a party can back, broker, or exploit.
- **The Old Forest** (tier 1–2) — the forbidden wood draws the curious; three things happen there, only one of them documented.

## At a glance

The Wealdvale is the breadbasket eorldom — rich, fat, lightly defended, and held by an eorl who wants only to be left alone. Its Witan vote is winnable: Osgar will lean to whichever faction credibly checks Count Edmund of the Hollowford March, the one subject that pierces his reluctance. Of its five houses, three refract the Edmund problem — Wenhill wants war on him, Millbrook bears his refugees, Faldon secretly serves him — while Wellow is the eorldom's one plainly honest house and Norwood is a contained succession-intrigue. Crisis stance: soft regency, conditional, and distractedly so. Bestiary groups operating here: the Road-Outlaws (4A) on the southern road and the Arrival-Hunters (A1).
