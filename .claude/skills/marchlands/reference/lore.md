This file traces the layered past of the Marchlands: the rise and fall of Aureoth, the post-imperial breakaway that produced the Marchland regions, and Hethrun's own founding within that frame. Three nested timelines; what happened earlier shapes what is happening now.

## The Aureothan Empire (rise, peak, fall)

### Origin

Aureoth began as a conquering army gone native. Roughly twelve hundred years before the current date, an army out of the south invaded the lands that would become the imperial north and the imperial east — and stayed. The invaders adopted the climate, the agriculture, the older sacred sites; they intermarried with such of the original inhabitants as they did not drive into exile. Within two centuries the conquerors had become Aureothans and the conquered had largely disappeared.

The original inhabitants of these lands were **driven into exile**. Some fled west into what is now the Silvering verge and were absorbed by the elves over slow centuries. Some fled north into the White Mountains and made compact with the dwarves to be permitted to live in the lower foothills (the descendants of these refugees survived as small communities until the Aureothan collapse, when most rejoined the wider human world). Some were driven *under* — into bog-fastnesses, into caves, into the barrows of the Heath. Those who hid under the Heath are still there in some form. The Veiled Mother priesthood remembers them; the Codex Church does not.

### Rise crisis

Aureoth's rise was instigated by a resource exhaustion crisis. The new Aureothan civilization, in its third century, had farmed its frontier provinces past sustainability and faced widespread famine. The crisis was overcome by **faith strengthening them against the woe** — specifically, the elevation of the old conquering-warrior cult into a full state religion that imposed rotation-and-fallow rituals, redistribution of land, and the absorption of frontier populations into a coherent imperial faith. This proto-Codex faith was what eventually fragmented into the modern Codex Church after Aureoth fell.

### Peak

Aureoth's peak was its golden age of architecture. The capital city at Caerendis (which Aurea now rules) was built in this era — vast basilicas, the great walls that have never fallen, the road network that still threads through every Marchland region. **Eldermouth's Aureothan foundations** date to the late peak: the city was an imperial port-administration center for the northern peninsula, and the walls of present-day Eldermouth are imperial work on the Aureothan footprint. Most Marchland towns sit on Aureothan-era infrastructure.

The College of Iron in Aurea was founded in the Aureothan peak as the imperial school of arcane practice. Its current curriculum descends from that origin. Master Aldwin trained there — at an institution older than every Marchland kingdom combined.

### Fall

Aureoth fell to two compounding crises five centuries before the current date.

The first crisis was **internal refugees from disaster** — a sequence of climate-and-plague events on Aureoth's southern frontier that displaced two million people northward into the imperial heartland over a single generation. The empire's redistribution-and-fallow systems, descended from the rise-crisis solutions, could not handle population pressure at this scale. Cities became overcrowded and starving.

The second crisis came too soon. While the empire was still managing the southern displacement, a **second, distinct crisis** struck the eastern provinces: this was the original incursion of the people who became the modern Marchland-region populations (the proto-Saxons, proto-Slavs, proto-Mississippian-coded, proto-Igbo/Yoruba-coded, etc. — multiple migrations from beyond the imperial borders, simultaneously, none of which the empire would have noticed individually but which together arrived just as the southern refugee crisis was peaking). The empire could not respond to both at once. It was actually two crises, and it was too much.

The eastern half of Aureoth held together under reformed imperial leadership and became Ostmark (which calls itself Aureoth-the-True). The western half fragmented. The northern provinces — the Marchlands — broke into the six regional polities under various incomer leadership in the chaos. The Codex Church survived the collapse barely, by abandoning its imperial state-religion role and reorganizing as a transnational institution under the Patriarch in what would become Aurea.

### What persists of Aureoth

- **Architecture.** Walls, foundations, road networks, basilicas. Eldermouth on Aureothan walls. Most Marchland capitals on Aureothan footprints. The road from Cynesburh to Eldermouth is laid imperial stone for the first hundred miles.
- **Law.** Aureothan codified law underlies modern Marchland law in most regions. Wessmark law is the most direct descendant; Hethrun law is a Norse overlay on Aureothan substrate; Volotrad law is the loosest descent. The Codex Patriarchate preserves the law-books.
- **Faith.** The Codex Church directly descends from the Aureothan state cult; it is the empire's surviving institutional remnant. The Burning One in Ostmark is also descended (more obscurely) from one of the Aureothan state cult's saints.
- **Coin.** Aureothan-stamp gold coins still circulate; they are highly valued for the silver content and for the imperial stamp, which the Codex blesses. Most major trade transactions use Aureothan coin alongside the current Marchland mints.
- **Buried.** Aureothan ruins are everywhere. The Heath, the Greatwood verge, the Iron Strait islands, the Volotradan river-confluences, the Tihatchee old-watershed, the Vellanaadu archipelago's largest island — every Marchland region has Aureothan ruins suitable for adventure. Some are empty. Some are not.

## The Marchland breakaway

### Origin

The Marchlands as a coherent grouping emerged after Aureoth's fall, **united by faith or ideology** — specifically, by a proto-Codex movement that held together at first because the surviving imperial faithful needed each other. The six regions formed around the incomer leaderships of the post-imperial migrations, but the *grouping* of the six as "Marchlands" rather than separate kingdoms was a Codex-driven coordinating fiction in the first century after Aureoth.

The original inhabitants (the Aureothan-era populations who had been imperial subjects, not the pre-Aureothan ancients) **retained small areas of self-rule** under the new incomer regimes. Wessmark's Codex bishopric of Aelmsbury sits on what was an Aureothan-era civic-administration center that was permitted to continue functioning under Saxon overlordship. Similar arrangements exist (or existed) in the other regions.

### Rise crisis

The Marchlands' rise crisis was **religious or ideological excess**. The proto-Codex movement that held the breakaway regions together in the first century became radical: militant evangelism, forced conversions of incomer leaderships, mass burnings of pre-Codex shrines, particularly in Wessmark and what would become Hethrun. The crisis split the Codex itself.

The Marchlands overcame this through **industrious labor and tireless exertion** — specifically, a long rebuilding effort led by the Codex moderates who broke from the militants, restored the older pre-Aureothan accommodations, and built up the regional governments under their own incomer dynasties. This took most of two centuries (the third and fourth centuries after Aureoth's fall). It is what produced the modern Marchland identity.

### Peak

The Marchlands' peak was the founding of the great academies — institutions that are still respected. The College of Iron in Aurea was founded in this era (rebuilt from Aureothan ruins of the original imperial school). The Solar Academy in Ostmark dates similarly. The Stone-Lama oracular tradition in the White Mountains was first formalized for human consultation in this period; the Silvering opened limited diplomatic contact with the human Marchland regions in this period; the Codex Patriarchate established its current Aurean seat in this period.

### Current crisis

The Marchlands' current crisis is **ideological divide** — the multi-way fracture among the Codex (orthodox), the Codex (heterodox / Aurean-aligned), the Old Faith resurgence in several regions, the Burning One penetration from Ostmark, and the rising influence of the Marchland-neighbor faiths (Volotradan thunderer worship, Iyendu divine-king assertion, Tihatchee dragon-cult). The risk is that **decadence and self-absorption** dooms the current order — that the Marchland regions are too distracted by internal religious-political conflict to respond when external pressure finally lands.

The High King at Wessmark sees this clearly. He is one of the few who does.

## Hethrun's founding

### Origin

Hethrun specifically was founded by **refugees from a fallen land** — the original Norse who came to the north peninsula three centuries ago. Their own homeland (a Norse-coded coastal realm further north, beyond the White Mountains' northern shoulder, on a frozen sea) had collapsed under combined climate-and-invasion crises. Three fleets of Norse refugees made landfall on the north peninsula and fought their way ashore.

The peninsula's original inhabitants were Saxon — Aureothan-era settlers who had become established peninsula-natives in the four hundred years since the empire fell, ruling under their own thane-and-eorl system, Codex-influenced but Old-Faith-grounded. The Norse refugees **made the Saxons an oppressed underclass** for the first generation, ruling as a conquering minority over the agricultural population. Over the next two centuries the layers partially fused: intermarriage, cultural absorption, the Norse adopting Saxon place-names and farming practices, the Saxons adopting Norse warband structure and Raven-cult.

### Rise crisis

Hethrun's rise crisis (the second-generation Norse occupation) was a **barbarian invasion** — specifically, a wave of unknown raiders out of the deep north who landed in Stonebay one hundred and seventy years ago and burned half the peninsula's coastal settlements over a six-year campaign. These raiders were never identified definitively; they may have been a fragment of the same Norse population that originally drove Hethrun's founders out of their homeland, pursuing them across the centuries; they may have been something else entirely.

The crisis was overcome through **ruthless but effective sacrifices**. The second King Aelfric (great-grandfather to the recently-deceased King Aelfric the Just) sacrificed an entire fyrd at a coastal pass to delay the raiders long enough for the kingdom's other forces to muster. He led the fyrd personally and died at it. The raiders were eventually pushed back into the sea. The mass grave of the sacrificed fyrd is on the Heath, marked by a single great cairn; this is one of the Heath's barrow-sites and Hethrun fyrdmen still pour ale-libations on its stones.

### Peak

Hethrun's peak — the long generation before the current crisis — produced **magnificent works of art**. The royal hall at Eldermouth was rebuilt in this period with carved stone reliefs, the Kingsmound's enclosure was elaborated, the great Codex monastery at Hollowford was endowed and decorated, and the skaldic tradition produced its best verse. The peak culminated in King Aelfric the Just (the recently-deceased king), who was a competent administrator and Codex patron during a stable peace.

### Current crisis

Hethrun's current crisis is **ideological divide** — Codex versus Old Faith versus Burning One — combined with the **succession failure** that opens the campaign. The kingdom's strengths (fyrd-based military, skaldic political coordination, Codex administrative capacity) are not the strengths needed for the current problem. Hethrun's strengths are **useless against the problem**.

## What the GM should hold

The history is layered: pre-Aureoth (the exiled peoples now under the Heath), Aureoth (the imperial period whose ruins are everywhere), post-Aureoth (the breakaway and the Marchlands), and recent (Hethrun's own three centuries). Every location has at least one layer that could surface in play. Eldermouth's walls are Aureothan. Master Aldwin trained at an institution older than every Marchland kingdom. The mass-grave cairn on the Heath is Hethrun's. The Heath barrows themselves are older still.

The **two-crisis fall of Aureoth** is the central structural fact of the world. The Marchlands exist because Aureoth could not respond to two simultaneous pressures. The Marchlands are about to face their own multi-pressure crisis — Codex/Old Faith/Burning One ideological divide, plus Aurean tribute pressure, plus Ostmark infiltration, plus Vaerelloth stirring, plus the elven recruitment, plus the dragon-cult, plus the boy-king. The historical echo is deliberate; the GM should let the players notice.

The **disappeared Tihatchee emissaries** are seeded here for the GM. One is dead; the other is at Brackenford with Mildryd. If the players pull on that thread early, the dragon-cult enters play sooner.

The **Codex relic-courier disappearance** is also seeded — somewhere on the Hollowford road, something happened to a Codex traveler. The Burning One cult, the dragon-cult, an Aurean intelligence faction, or barrow-folk could all plausibly be responsible.

## Cosmology overview

The Marchlands hold a layered cosmology rather than an organized one. Multiple faiths coexist, contradict, and compete; most ordinary people honor different gods for different occasions, picking whichever fits the work at hand. Sky-Father for oaths, Veiled Mother for childbirth and healing, the Raven for the dead, the Codex for cities and southern trade, and the Burning One increasingly for grievance. The educated treat this as syncretism; the orthodox of any single faith treat it as faithlessness. Both are right.

Souls: the Codex teaches a judgement-and-station afterlife where the worthy join the Codex's higher beings and the unworthy diminish. The Old Faith (Sky-Father, Raven, Veiled Mother together) teaches that souls go where their oaths and burials send them — proper rites matter immensely. The Burning One teaches that vengeance unfulfilled in life follows the soul outward and inward, and that those who completed their vengeance burn brightly forever while those who did not become dimming embers. Most ordinary people believe some combination of all three and worry about all three.

Demihuman pantheons (elven, dwarven, the Chola river-god, Volotradan thunderer, Iyendu divine king, Tihatchee sun-falcon) operate within their own peoples and are treated as foreign by Marchland humans. Codex priests would call them devils or saints depending on theology; Old Faith folk are more inclined to consider them real gods of other peoples.

## The Old Faith — Sky-Father, the Raven, the Veiled Mother

These three are the older Marchland gods, predating Aureothan conquest in their oldest forms and absorbing significant Norse layers when Hethrun's ancestors arrived. They are not formally a pantheon; their priesthoods do not coordinate; they are grouped together because they share a common opposition to the Codex Church.

### Sky-Father

**Domains:** Theft or Crime + Sun, Moon, or Stars.

**Origin:** Sky-Father was originally a saint in an older pre-Aureothan faith — a mortal hero of resistance against the Aureothan conquering army, posthumously deified by the conquered peoples and absorbed by their Saxon successors as the principal sky-god. When the Norse arrived three centuries ago they brought their own sky-god and the two fused; the Hethrun Sky-Father today combines the older Saxon hero-god with the Norse all-father in a way that priests of both older traditions consider partially mistaken.

**Function:** Patron of an illegal but inevitable trade — specifically, *raiding*. Sky-Father is the witness-god under the open sky, and he is patron of those who take by right of arms what others would withhold by law. He is also patron of oaths, because both raids and oaths are made under his celestial witness.

**Worshipped by:** Norse warriors, Saxon thanes, traveling merchants, anyone swearing serious oaths, and any Hethrun raider before a voyage.

**Sacred requirement:** Make particular sacrifices at certain times — specifically, the first-take of every raid is owed to Sky-Father. Raiders pour a portion of plunder onto a stone-altar at journey's end. Oath-takers swear under open sky; an oath sworn under a roof binds only weakly.

**Common invocations:** "By the Star-Eye," "Under the open witness," "By the Sky-Father's reckoning." Curses: "May the sky see you," "May the stars name your name."

**One distinctive practice:** The **oath-ring**. Every Norse-blooded Hethrun warrior wears a silver arm-ring; each oath kept earns a notch on the ring. A warrior whose oath-ring has many notches is trusted with serious oaths; one whose ring is unmarked is offered no serious vow.

**Cleric domains in 5.5e mechanical interface:** Light (the celestial witness), War (the sanction of raids), Trickery (the patron of justified theft). Paladins of Sky-Father take Oath of the Watchers (the celestial witness) or Oath of Vengeance (for raids of legitimate redress).

### The Raven

**Domains:** Theft or Crime + Sun, Moon, or Stars.

**Origin:** The Raven was constructed — built as a god by an ancient war-cult of the original pre-Aureothan peoples and persisted past its creators. The cult that made the Raven is gone; the Raven remains. Modern Raven-priests inherit a god whose architects they cannot identify and whose original purpose may not have been what the current cult thinks it was.

**Function:** Provides good harvests and human fertility — but in the Raven's peculiar way. The Raven is the carrion-eater who turns death into fed life. Fields fertilized by buried dead; children born after fathers fall in raids; the cycle of taking-and-leaving. Raven priests bless the harvest by ritually feeding the dead to the new soil. They preside over both childbirth (the bloody coming-out) and burial (the bloody going-back).

**Worshipped by:** Hethrun common folk widely; skalds especially (the Raven sees what is taken and what is left, and the skald who watches sees as the Raven sees); midwives; sexton-priests of Norse-influenced funerary practice.

**Sacred requirement:** Never cooperate with the Codex Church. Raven-priests refuse to enter Codex temples, refuse Codex blessings on the dead, refuse joint ceremonies. The Codex returns the refusal. This is one of the central religious fractures of the Marchlands.

**Common invocations:** "The Raven sees," "By the Raven's eye." Sworn upon by skalds especially. Sigevard the Silver-Tongue is devoted to the Raven, treats his Raven-devotion as license to break promises freely — because the Raven sees what is taken, including a broken word, and the Raven judges only the watching, not the act.

**One distinctive practice:** Carrion-feasts at battle-sites. Three days after a battle, Raven-priests come to the site and oversee the proper exposure of the bodies — feeding the ravens that come to feed on the slain, then collecting the cleaned bones for proper burial or pyre. This is sacred work. Norse warriors who fall fighting expect this rite.

**Cleric domains:** Death (carrion-and-rebirth), Grave, Trickery (the watcher who sees what is taken).

### The Veiled Mother

**Domains:** Music + One or More Seasons.

**Origin:** Veiled Mother simply always existed since creation. She is older than any of the other Marchland gods; her priesthood predates Aureoth, the Saxons, and the Norse. Her name has shifted across languages but her function has not. The pre-Aureothan peoples drove into exile under the Heath when Aureoth conquered — they were her priesthood originally. What survives of them under the barrows is part of why her cult retains such depth.

**Function:** Provides healing and protection from danger. The Veiled Mother is the healer-god, the chant that mends, the autumn song that closes the year and opens the next.

**Worshipped by:** Cunning-folk like Gythwin; midwives (alongside the Raven, in dual rite); herbalists; rural Marchlanders broadly; many people of all faiths in private when sick.

**Sacred requirement:** Forbid a certain common profession — specifically, **mining**. The Veiled Mother's faithful do not break the earth's body for metal. The Heath's bog-iron is exempted by ancient compromise (it surrenders willingly to be drawn from bog-water and was never dug), but actual mining is forbidden. This creates serious tension with the Codex bishop in Eldermouth (the Codex blesses mining as legitimate wealth-creation), with the College of Iron in Aurea, and with Volotradan amber-trade interests.

**Common invocations:** "Mother veil me," "By the autumn song." Sworn upon by women in childbirth, by healers before any work, by herbalists harvesting.

**One distinctive practice:** The autumn **chant of the Going-Out** — sung at every funeral by women trained in it, a song that has been passed down for so long that no one knows what the older words mean. Cunning-women like Gythwin train apprentices in the chant for years. Singing it badly is considered worse than not singing it at all.

**Cleric domains:** Life, Nature, Twilight. Druids of the Veiled Mother form the Circle of the Veiled Mother (one of two named druidic circles in the Marchlands; see `factions.md`).

## The Codex

**Domains:** A Human Vice (Avarice, specifically) + A Local Terrain Type (highways and roads).

**Origin:** The Codex's central figure was a saint of an earlier faith — specifically, an Aureothan-era state cult that the Codex's founders fragmented from in the imperial collapse. The Codex preserves the Aureothan road-blessing traditions, the imperial counting-houses, the literate liturgy in Aureothan script. Three saints of the older faith were elevated to godhood in the Codex's first century; the central figure is now worshipped as the Codex itself rather than by his prior name.

**Function:** Bestows material wealth upon petitioners. The Codex is the god of prosperity-as-blessing, organized commerce, regulated avarice transformed into faith. The Codex Patriarchate is genuinely wealthy and its faithful tend to prosper. Codex priests live well.

**Worshipped by:** Merchants, kings (officially), Codex monks and priests, anyone who wants to be considered respectable in town. The faith of choice for the social-climbing, the prosperous, and those who want to be either.

**Sacred requirement:** Never cooperate with a certain faith — the **Old Faith**, specifically the Veiled Mother. The Codex refuses joint ceremonies, refuses to recognize Veiled Mother burials as proper, refuses to permit Veiled Mother symbols on Codex consecrated ground. This refusal is mutual but the Codex enforces it more aggressively. Sister Aelfwyn at Hollowford has been carefully ignoring the prohibition for fifteen years and the bishop of Eldermouth has not yet moved against her.

**Common invocations:** "By the Codex's count," "Under the holy ledger," "By the great gates." Curses: "May your name be struck from the rolls."

**One distinctive practice:** The **weekly accounting** — Codex faithful are expected to account weekly to their priest for their week's transactions, both honest and dishonest. Honest dealings are blessed; dishonest dealings are confessed and made right (or notional restitution paid to the Codex). This is genuinely useful pastoral care and is also why the Codex Church has the best financial intelligence of any institution in the Marchlands.

**Cleric domains:** Order (the ledger), Forge (regulated industry), Trickery (the regulation of necessary deceptions), Knowledge (the literate liturgy). Paladins of the Codex take Oath of Devotion (the orthodox), Oath of the Crown (the state-aligned), or rarely Oath of the Watchers.

**Irrational hatred + Up From the Gutter (faction context).** The Codex Church as an institution has an Irrational Hatred tag — specifically of the Veiled Mother cult, beyond what the doctrinal differences justify. Some of the senior Codex bishops know *why* the hatred is irrational: it was deliberately implanted in the Codex's foundation by the surviving Aureothan-era priests who feared that without an enemy the new faith would not cohere. The Patriarch in Aurea is one of the few who knows; the rest carry the hatred without origin. Severin of Eldermouth carries it without knowing why. The Up From the Gutter tag reflects the Codex's actual founding: it was a small new faith in Aureoth's collapse and clawed its way to its current prominence over five centuries.

## The Burning One — Ostmark's solar imperial cult

**Domains:** A Human Vice (Wrath, specifically) + A Local Terrain Type (volcanic vents and sacred flame-sites).

**Origin:** The Burning One is the genius of a particular land or location — specifically, of a volcanic mountain in eastern Ostmark called **Pyrhalion**, where a flame has burned continuously for as long as records exist. The deity was originally local; the Aureothan-era state cult absorbed it; the Ostmark continuator state has elevated it to imperial faith.

**Function:** Empowers those seeking revenge for wrongs. The Burning One is the god of vengeance, the patron of those who have been done wrong and who require fire to right the wrong. State application: Ostmark uses the Burning One as foreign policy — finding wronged people across the Marchlands and equipping them to act. Branwald's quiet patience in Stonebay is Burning One work: he is *finding the right people*. He has not converted anyone openly because conversion is not the point; empowerment is. He has been sounding out who has a deep grudge and who would be useful equipped with one.

**Worshipped by:** Officially, all Ostmark subjects; in practice, those with serious grievances broadly across the Marchlands. The cult attracts the ambitious wronged.

**Sacred requirement:** Make particular sacrifices at certain times — specifically, fire-sacrifices at sun's standstill (solstices) and at specific stellar conjunctions visible from Pyrhalion. Branwald has performed two of these in private on the Stonebay headland; one observed (at the new moon) was noted in the existing canon, and there has been at least one other.

**Common invocations:** "By the burning eye," "Under the standstill flame." Sworn upon by the wronged who intend to act.

**One distinctive practice:** The **vengeance-pact** — the cult's central ritual. A wronged petitioner names their grievance, names their target, and accepts a burning brand on their forearm. The brand never fully heals (one of the in-world signs of Burning One devotion). The petitioner thereafter has Burning One backing — minor wizardry, contacts, occasional material support — until the vengeance is fulfilled. Pact-breakers (those who renounce their vengeance) are burned in literal fire by the cult. Pact-fulfillers (those who complete their vengeance) are honored, regardless of the cost.

**Cleric domains:** Light (the flame), War (sanctioned vengeance), Order (the pact-binding). Warlock patron: Fiend pact (the deeper face of the Burning One has an infernal-tier aspect older than Ostmark; Branwald is technically a warlock with a fiend pact, though he calls it priestly).

**Hidden Origins + Persecuting Foe (faction context).** The Burning One has hidden origins — the cult does not advertise that Pyrhalion's continuous flame was lit by an Aureothan-era apotheosized sorcerer, not a "primordial sacred fire." The Persecuting Foe is the Codex (mutually). Branwald carries the doctrinal-historical truth privately; he uses it carefully.

## The Marchland-neighbor gods (in brief)

The other Marchland regions each have their own dominant deities. These are real, are honored in their home regions, and would be present in any Marchland Witan or inter-region negotiation. Hethrun folk are vaguely aware of them.

### Voloth, the Volotradan Thunderer

**Domains:** Healing + Forests.

The thunder-god of Volotrad — local genius of the great forest, patron of harvest-rain, healer-through-storm. His priesthood is the **shtervochi**, forest-monks who tend sacred oaks and call thunder ritually. Voloth provides good harvests through proper rain and protects against forest-illnesses. **Sacred requirement:** never eat mushroom of any kind (Voloth's quarrel with the Forest Mother is mediated by the mushroom-taboo). **Cleric domains:** Life, Tempest, Nature.

### Leshmati, the Volotradan Forest Mother

**Domains:** Agriculture + Beauty or Sex.

The other primary Volotradan deity — primordial, always-existed, cosmological mother who explains creation and order. Her cult is older than the thunderer's. She is honored at field-edges, at woman-rites, at planting and harvest. **Sacred requirement:** significant tithe at harvest — the first sheaf of every field is hers. **Cleric domains:** Life, Nature, Twilight.

### Obasi-Ezi, the Iyendu Divine King

**Domains:** Agriculture + Beauty or Sex.

The deified founder-king of the Iyendu line, embodied in each current Eze. Empowers those seeking revenge for wrongs done to the kingdom or to the ancestral order — Obasi-Ezi is patron of *just* revenge for proper grievances. **Sacred requirement:** honor smiths (smith-caste is sacred under Obasi-Ezi); degrade slavers (Iyendu refuses slavery, and an Obasi-Ezi devotee cannot countenance enslavement of any human). **Cleric domains:** Life, War, Order. The Eze Iyendu is a working channel of Obasi-Ezi when he sits in ritual judgment.

### The Sun-Falcon Atohi-Eseth

**Domains:** Ruling + Storms or Rain.

The principle of "rightful watchfulness from above" reified as a deity, worshipped at Tihatchee mound-capital. Patron of paramount rulers and storm-bringing. **Sacred requirement:** honor stargazers and astronomer-priests (Mississippian calendar-keepers are sacred); degrade cave-dwellers (the Sun-Falcon's faithful disdain those who live in earth-holes; this is part of why the Mtosi cult's transition to dragon-worship was so theologically jarring — Vaerelloth lives in caves). **Cleric domains:** Light, Tempest, Order.

### Kaveri-Suvar, the Chola River God

**Domains:** Theft or Crime + Sun, Moon, or Stars.

A minor deity of the Chola pantheon; protector of the Chola diaspora specifically — guardian of those who carry the river-god's water in flasks far from the river itself. Worshipped by the Stonebay Chola merchant quarter and by the Vellanaadu protectorate's smaller temples. **Sacred requirement:** never cooperate with the Burning One (the Chola dharmic faith specifically refuses Burning One worship). **Cleric domains:** Knowledge, Order, Trickery.

## Tensions

The religious tensions in the Marchlands are fundamental. The GM should foreground them.

- **Codex versus Veiled Mother.** Mutual non-cooperation by sacred requirement on both sides. Codex says Veiled Mother forbids mining and thus opposes prosperity; Veiled Mother says Codex blesses earth-violation. In practice this means the Codex bishop in Eldermouth has not spoken to Gythwin in twenty years and Sister Aelfwyn at Hollowford has been hiding her syncretism for fifteen.
- **Codex versus the Raven.** Mutual non-cooperation. Raven-priests will not enter Codex temples; Codex will not bless the war-dead under Raven rite. Sigevard the Silver-Tongue is a Raven devotee at a court increasingly Codex-aligned.
- **Burning One versus Codex.** The Burning One is officially heretical under Codex doctrine. Father Severin would arrest Branwald immediately if he could prove what Branwald is; he cannot, and Branwald has diplomatic standing. This is a low-grade clock.
- **Burning One versus Chola river-god.** Specific doctrinal incompatibility (the Chola explicitly refuse cooperation). The Stonebay Chola merchant quarter is wary of Branwald; he has visited twice and they were polite and gave nothing.
- **Sky-Father old form versus new form.** The Wessmark Sky-Father is the older saint-promoted-to-god; the Hethrun Sky-Father is the Norse-fused all-father. Devotees of the old form (in Wessmark) regard the Hethrun form as half-pagan; devotees of the Hethrun form regard the old form as half-civilized. This rarely matters but flares at coronations and great oaths.

## Outlawed or hidden faiths

### The Mtosi Dragon-Cult (Vaerelloth-veneration)

The dragon-cult in the Tihatchee region (specifically the Lost-Sun Chiefdom of Mtosi) worships Vaerelloth as a sun-replacement. Officially the cult is heretical from the Tihatchee Sun-Falcon perspective and unknown to the rest of the Marchlands. The cult offers: real magical favors (Vaerelloth grants them), real protection (no other power dares move on Mtosi), and a coherent theology that has weathered three generations.

What devotees must do: ritual fires fed with copper-and-shell (the older Sun-Falcon's wealth, now offered to Vaerelloth instead), seasonal pilgrimage to Vaerelloth's outer caves in the White Mountains' southern foothills, and an oath that "the sun above is false; the smoke-wing is the true watcher." Children born to Mtosi families are bound to the cult from infancy.

Why mainstream society fears them: most do not yet know they exist. The Suncatcher of Tihatchee knows but is constrained. The dwarves know and are furious about it. The Silvering elves know and are recruiting agents to deal with it. Vaerelloth's recent wing-stirring is partially driven by the cult's escalating ritual demands.

### Older barrow-cult under the Heath

Beneath the Heath barrows in Hethrun, what is left of the pre-Aureothan exiled peoples persists in some form. Gythwin knows what she can bear to know about them. The cult — if it can be called that — is not actively worshipping anything; it is hibernating. But the Veiled Mother priesthood maintains certain ancient observances at certain Heath sites because not maintaining them would *wake the barrow-folk*. The Codex Church does not believe in the barrow-folk and considers Veiled Mother observance at the Heath sites superstition; this is one of the Codex's key errors in the Marchlands.

## How to invoke divinity in play

For the AI Game Master:

- NPCs of any standing should reference at least one god in oaths, curses, or stress responses. A Hethrun fyrdman will swear "By the Star-Eye"; a Norse-blooded raider will add "and the Raven sees"; a Codex merchant will say "By the Codex's count"; a Veiled Mother cunning-woman will whisper "Mother veil me."
- Common folk swear by gods relevant to their station. A skald swears by the Raven. A midwife swears by the Veiled Mother. A merchant swears by the Codex. A raider swears by Sky-Father and Raven both.
- Sacred requirements are behavioral signatures. A devout follower of the Veiled Mother will never participate in mining or bless an iron-mine. A devout Raven-priest will never enter the Eldermouth Codex temple, even on a polite errand. A Burning One brand is visible if the sleeve is rolled. These are *fingerprints*; they identify devotion in ways characters cannot easily fake.
- The religious tensions surface in scenes through *choices*. A Codex bishop is asked to bless a fyrd levy that includes Raven-marked warriors and must decide whether to do so. A Veiled Mother cunning-woman is asked to heal a Codex bishop. A Burning One brand is visible on a man whose vengeance target is sitting at the same table.

## Aurea — the French-coded feudal kingdom to the south

### Essence

Aurea is the great feudal kingdom that emerged in the western core of fallen Aureoth, ruling from the imperial capital that still calls itself by its Aureothan name (Caerendis), wearing the Norman-French coding of a knightly aristocracy that conquered its way through Aureoth's collapse and then settled the survivors as their lords. Aurea is the Marchlands' main southern pressure.

### Government and culture

A feudal monarchy with strong vassal duchies and counties. The King of Aurea is **Aldric IV of House Volusen**, fifty-two years old, in his eighteenth year on the throne, contending with three rival claimant houses (House Tassard, House Marivell, House Caerellian) that recognize him formally and contest him constantly. House Volusen is the senior of the four claimant houses; the other three are positioning for the next succession.

Aurean culture is heraldic, tournament-going, courtly-loving in poetry, brutal in practice. Knights are sworn to lords; lords are sworn to dukes; dukes to the king. The College of Iron (Aurea's premier wizardry institution) trains wizards from across the Marchlands and the wider world. The Codex Church Patriarch sits in Aurea at the Aureothan-era basilica that has never fallen down.

### Why Aurea presses Hethrun

House Volusen treats the Marchlands as a portfolio of investment opportunities. Aurea conducted a survey of Marchland tribute arrears two years ago and identified Hethrun as a weak target — small kingdom, succession crisis pending, harbor on the Iron Strait that the king of Aurea would like for his own reasons. **Cassian Drey**, Aurea's envoy at Eldermouth, is a House Volusen man and almost certainly an intelligencer. The Aurean demand is for three years' tribute or "advisors" — which means a Volusen army stationed at Eldermouth to "stabilize" the kingdom on behalf of House Volusen interests.

### What Aurea actually wants

The Iron Strait harbor at Eldermouth, a marriage of Hadric (or whoever sits the Hethrun throne) into a Volusen cadet line, and Hethrun's tribute treated as permanent. Long-term, Aurea wants every petty Marchland kingdom as a Volusen client. The other claimant houses (Tassard especially) are running their own Marchland operations to compete.

### Hooks

- A House Tassard envoy arrives in Eldermouth, undercut Drey's offer with a counter-offer.
- A Volusen marriage proposal arrives for Hadric — and for Hadric to be moved south to Aurea for his "education."
- The Aurean College of Iron sends a senior wizard to "consult" with Master Aldwin; this wizard is a Tassard creature.
- Drey's gambling debts come due. He is in someone's pocket who is not House Volusen and the holder of those debts is asking for the wrong things.

## Ostmark — the Byzantine-coded eastern continuator

### Essence

Ostmark is what the Marchlands call the Byzantine-coded continuator state east of the Iron Strait. Ostmark calls itself **Aureoth-the-True** internally, in its own Greek-coded scholarly tongue, and considers itself the rightful continuation of the empire that "the western barbarians" let fall. It has held together for five centuries while Aurea fragmented. It is sophisticated, bureaucratic, expansionist, and patient.

### Government and culture

A sacred monarchy with a vast bureaucracy. The **Autokrator** sits in the imperial capital — a man named **Konstantinos IX Drosimenes**, sixty-one years old, twenty-third of his dynasty, a competent administrator who has stabilized his frontiers and turned his attention west. The court runs on protocol and intrigue in equal measure; the bureaucracy is staffed by examination, not patronage; the army is professional rather than feudal.

The state religion is the Burning One cult — the **Soliclast**, a vengeance-coded solar deity whose worship empowers those seeking revenge for wrongs. The Soliclast is the Burning One; the Marchland name is a translation. (See `lore.md`.) Burning One worship is the official faith; Codex worship is tolerated but disadvantaged; other faiths are persecuted.

The **Solar Academy** at the imperial capital trains battle-mages who serve in the imperial armies. Branwald the Star-Priest is a Solar Academy graduate. So are roughly four hundred other wizards currently serving Ostmark in various capacities.

### Why Ostmark presses Hethrun

Ostmark sees the western Marchlands as territory that *should* have been recovered when Aureoth fell — provinces that should have returned to the imperial fold and instead went feral under barbarian kinglets. Konstantinos IX has been pushing west diplomatically for fifteen years: cultural infiltration (Burning One missionaries), trade dominance (Ostmark silk and worked metal compete favorably with Aurean goods at Stonebay), and direct intelligence operations (Branwald and the network he is building).

Ostmark wants Hethrun as a client — but more importantly, as a beachhead for further western pressure. If Hethrun goes Burning One under Ranulf, Ostmark gains a harbor on the western shore of the Iron Strait. The next target after that is the Wessmark coast Danelaw enclaves.

### What Ostmark actually wants

Long-term: the recovery of the entire fallen empire. Short-term: a Ranulf-aligned Hethrun. Medium-term: a war with Aurea that breaks Aurean power in the south and lets Ostmark roll up the Marchlands while Aurea is occupied.

### Hooks

- A Solar Academy fire-mage arrives in Stonebay to "consult" with Branwald. This mage is more powerful than Branwald and is checking his work.
- An Ostmark trade fleet arrives at Stonebay with goods priced to wreck the Eldermouth market.
- A Burning One missionary moves inland to Brackenford, looking for wronged people to recruit.
- Branwald's working completes. (See `factions.md`.) Ostmark moves openly.

## The Silvering — elven peer realm

### Essence

The Silvering is the elven realm in the deep western forests, beyond the Greatwood verge at Mossdrowen. It has been there since before Aureoth. It is not aging like human realms age. The elves on the wood-roads at Mossdrowen are outliers — the Silvering's outer scouts, watchers, exiles, or messengers; the inner realm is a long week's march further west.

### Government and culture

The Silvering is ruled by a court whose precise structure no Marchlander has been allowed to see. The visible Silvering — what visitors might encounter — consists of high elven settlements among ancient trees, a martial order of forest-warders, scholar-priests of a tradition older than Aureoth, and craft-workers who produce items that the Aurean College of Iron has spent generations trying to replicate. Silvering elves live for centuries; some live for millennia.

Beneath the visible Silvering is the **faerie court of the Smokeless Throne** — an archfey court that the elves serve, tolerate, and fear in roughly equal measure. The faerie court is bound by accord older than the elves themselves, but the accord is *fraying*. The Last Survivor / Burnt Out tags on the Silvering as a faction (see `factions.md`) reflect this: the elves are exhausted from a multi-millennium vigil they cannot share with anyone outside their realm.

### Why the Silvering watches Hethrun

Two reasons. First, **Vaerelloth is stirring**, and the Silvering has older accords with the dragon than the dwarves have. The elves know more about what is happening in the White Mountains than they have told anyone. Second, **the dragon-cult in Tihatchee** is a problem the elves cannot solve themselves — the cult is far from Silvering territory and engaging directly would announce things the elves cannot afford to announce.

The elves on the wood-roads at Mossdrowen are *recruiting*. They are looking for someone who can act on their behalf in the dragon-cult matter without revealing the Silvering's hand.

### Hooks

- The elves approach Hagan formally with a request, framed as a favor returned for old service from his grandfather's grandfather.
- A Silvering exile (a faerie-court refugee who has chosen the elven side against the Smokeless Throne) appears in Eldermouth and seeks asylum.
- A faerie-court emissary appears on the wood-roads at Mossdrowen — not an elf, something *under* an elf, in elven shape. Hagan's scouts will know the difference; they will not survive to report it.
- An adventuring party that helps the elves with the dragon-cult problem can earn entry to the Silvering proper. Almost no human has done so in three hundred years.

## The White Mountains — Tibetan-coded dwarven kingdom

### Essence

The White Mountains rise to the north and northwest of the Marchlands. They are inhabited by two peoples: the dwarves and Vaerelloth. The dwarves have lived there for at least ten thousand years and possibly twenty. Vaerelloth has lived there for at least eight hundred years, possibly two thousand. They share the range under accord.

### The dwarves

The dwarves of the White Mountains are Tibetan-coded: monastic, oracular, contemplative, terrifyingly competent stoneworkers. Their realm is structured around twelve fortress-monasteries strung along the upper passes, each housing a contemplative order and a smithing tradition. The dwarven king-monk, the **Stone-Lama Vajra-of-the-Fourteenth-Pass**, holds office by oracular consensus rather than dynastic succession; he has held the seat for forty-three years and the oracles have not yet indicated his successor.

Dwarven culture: sky-burial (corpses are exposed on high platforms for the eagles), tantric metalcraft (forging that involves visualization of the finished work for weeks before the hammer falls), oracular state-religion (the king-monk and major decisions are determined by oracles, not voted), philosophical commitment to long-term thinking. They do not like haste. They distrust kings whose seats their oracles have not validated.

### The dwarven double life

The dwarven faction has a Double Life tag (see `factions.md`). Their public face is the contemplative monastic kingdom; their private face is the long-running treaty/correspondence with Vaerelloth that almost no dwarf below the seventh contemplative grade is permitted to know about. The accord is real; it is also fraying. The dwarven king-monk has been considering whether the time has come to break it.

### Why the dwarves watch Hethrun

The dwarves do not particularly care about Hethrun's succession. They care intensely about Vaerelloth's flight rumors and about the Mtosi dragon-cult. The cult is a violation of accord — Vaerelloth was supposed to maintain her isolation. The dwarves want the cult ended, but ending it themselves would require breaking accord. They are looking for human agents.

The dwarves also trade with Stonebay and (more quietly) with Volotrad. A small dwarven smith-quarter exists in Eldermouth's harbor district — forty households, mostly silver and copper craftworkers, none from the contemplative grades.

### Hooks

- A dwarven monk descends to Eldermouth as a "trade consultant" and is actually a recruiter looking for a party to handle the Mtosi cult.
- The dwarven smith-quarter in Eldermouth holds a festival; a contemplative-grade dwarf appears there unexpectedly and speaks privately with Master Aldwin.
- An ancient dwarven weapon is offered for sale in Stonebay — too good to be sold, almost certainly bait for whatever the seller wants.
- Vaerelloth herself sends an emissary to the dwarves. The accord is about to break.

## The Chola Empire

### Essence

The Chola Empire is a vast Tamil-coded maritime empire across the southern sea, with its capital at **Thanjavur-of-the-Sea** and a tributary system extending across multiple seas and continents. The Vellanaadu protectorate is the empire's northernmost reach. The Marchlands hear of the Empire mostly through the protectorate, the merchant quarter in Stonebay, and the occasional Chola adventurer or scholar passing through.

### Government and culture

A monarchy of god-kings, where the king (**Maharaja Rajendra IX**) is considered partially divine in a dharmic-coded faith that integrates kingship into cosmological order. The Empire is administered through temple-administrations rather than feudal vassalage: temples manage land, collect taxes, perform ritual, and exercise local justice. Mercantile guilds operate with private armies under royal charter; the wealthiest guilds have their own war-dhows. Bronze, ivory, spice, silk, and worked stone are the Empire's primary exports.

The Chola pantheon is rich and varied; the river-god worshipped by the Stonebay quarter is one minor deity in a far broader cosmology. Chola dharmic faith broadly tolerates other faiths but specifically refuses cooperation with the Burning One — a doctrinal incompatibility the Solar Academy considers a personal insult.

### Why Chola cares about the Marchlands

The Empire's interests in the Marchlands run through Vellanaadu. The protectorate is a strategic foothold for further northern trade and a check on Norse piracy. Vellanaadu also sources amber, fur, worked iron, and certain alchemical materials that the Empire wants. The Empire is currently undecided about whether to commit more force to wipe out Skarrgaard's Norse pirates or to negotiate; a court faction is pushing for negotiation, another for elimination, and the Maharaja has not yet decided. This is the Ticking Bomb clock on the Chola protectorate faction.

### Hooks

- A Chola war-fleet arrives at Vellanaadu unexpectedly. Either Skarrgaard's days are numbered or someone has just made a major diplomatic error.
- A Chola scholar arrives in Eldermouth seeking ancient Aureothan texts — specifically, texts about Vaerelloth. He will pay well and ask politely.
- The Stonebay Chola merchant quarter receives a letter from Vellanaadu calling all loyal Chola back south. The Codex bishop notices.
- A Chola mercantile guild offers an adventuring contract to clear a specific island of Norse pirates; the contract is open to any party in good standing.

## The Sandseas — Sahel-coded caravan kingdoms

### Essence

South of Aurea, beyond the great Aurean watershed, lies the Sandseas: a Sahel-coded region of caravan kingdoms, divine kings with sacred-snake protectors, hereditary smith-castes, griot historian orders, and gold-and-salt trade routes that thread through deep desert and savanna. The Sandseas are not unified; they are a constellation of kingdoms, the most powerful being **Wagadou-of-the-Salt-Roads** and **Mali-of-the-Singers**.

### Why the Marchlands care

Indirectly. Sandseas gold flows north through Aurea; Sandseas griots occasionally appear at Aurean courts as guests of state; Sandseas caravan-warriors have served in Aurean armies as auxiliary cavalry for two generations. From the Marchlands perspective, the Sandseas are a distant power whose influence comes through Aurean intermediaries. A Sandseas merchant in Eldermouth would be a rare sight; one has not been seen in living memory.

### Hooks

- An Aurean knight arrives with a Sandseas auxiliary cavalry contingent attached to his retinue. They are paid through Aurea and answer to no one local.
- A Sandseas griot arrives in Eldermouth carrying a message that names someone in the Hethrun court. He will not say what the message is until he sees the named person.

## The Jade Throne — Chinese-coded vast eastern empire

### Essence

Far to the east beyond Ostmark, beyond mountains and steppes that few Marchlanders have ever seen, lies the Jade Throne: a Chinese-coded empire of sophisticated urban civilization, scholarly bureaucracy, paper-and-ink magical tradition, and population in the tens of millions. The Jade Throne considers itself the civilized world by its own measure; everyone outside is barbarian.

### Why the Marchlands care

Almost not at all. The Jade Throne's interest in the Marchlands is essentially zero; the Marchlands' interest in the Jade Throne is mostly reputational and material — jade ornaments occasionally reach Marchland courts as gifts of state, silk reaches Stonebay through Ostmark intermediaries at significant markup. A scholar from the Jade Throne might appear in Aurea once a decade; in the Marchlands, perhaps once a generation.

### Hooks

- A Jade Throne scholar-diplomat passes through Eldermouth on his way north to the White Mountains, carrying an inquiry from his own emperor to the Stone-Lama. He is interesting for what he knows about the broader world; he is dangerous because he reports back faithfully.
- A Jade Throne magical text arrives in Master Aldwin's library through obscure trade channels. Aldwin cannot read it but suspects it contains something he urgently wants to know.

## The Sunrise Isles — Heian-Japan-coded far empire

### Essence

Beyond the Jade Throne, across a great eastern sea, lie the Sunrise Isles: a Heian-Japan-coded far empire of aesthetic court culture, syncretic Shinto-Buddhist faith, emerging warrior aristocracy, color-coded court dress, and women-as-literary-elite. The Sunrise Isles are known in the Marchlands only by reputation: poetry that has reached Aurea through three intermediaries, a particular style of folding-fan that Aurean fashion has briefly adopted twice in the past century, rumors of an aesthetic sophistication that the West has nothing to match.

### Why the Marchlands care

Cultural prestige, mostly. A Sunrise Isles object in a court collection is a status item. A Sunrise Isles visitor in the Marchlands has not happened in any living person's memory; it would be a generational event.

### Hooks

- A scroll case arrives at Eldermouth's library by mysterious means. The scroll is from the Sunrise Isles, in a language no one local can read, addressed by name to "the dragon-blood heir of the northern peninsula." The heir is Hadric.
- A traveler appears in Hethrun claiming to be from the Sunrise Isles. The PCs may believe or disbelieve them; either way, what they want is the question.
