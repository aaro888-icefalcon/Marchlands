# Setting

The Marchlands frame at two scales: the tonal compass (what kind of world this is, what register the GM holds across sessions, what makes it un-generic), and the political-geographic overview (the six culturally-themed Marchland regions, the High Kingship at Wessmark, cultural enclaves crossing borders, inter-region politics at session zero). Macro frame; rarely retrieved per turn but should travel together when retrieved at all.

## Setting Compass

### What this world is

The Marchlands are humanity's frontier on a high-fantasy world where the Aureothan Empire fell five centuries ago and the broken provinces never reunited. Hethrun is one of the major Marchland powers — a Norse-on-Saxon coastal realm of some two million souls on the north peninsula, three weeks' hard riding from the High King's seat at Wessmark, three days' sail from Ostmark across the Iron Strait, and a long week's march from the elven Silvering through the Greatwood. Until lately its king had risen to Marshal of the High King and was rallying a Marchlands-wide invasion of Ostmark; he died suddenly, and the realm is now tipping into civil war. Adventure here lives in three overlapping registers, and the GM should hold all three at once.

### The three registers

**Feudal/noble jousting.** Houses have begun to develop. Eorls swear to the king, the king swears (nominally) to the High King at Wessmark, marriages are negotiated for years, oath-rings count the oaths kept, and a skald who sings against you in three halls has done you measurable political damage. Across the Marchlands the registers vary — Wessmark plays its succession-and-tribute games in pure-Saxon terms, Aurea to the south has gone fully Norman-feudal with knights and tournaments, the Volotrad principalities work through druzhina oaths to their princes, Iyendu's divine king brokers between his ancestor-bound oba-vassals, and the Chola protectorate in the southern archipelago runs on mercantile-guild charters. The Codex Church plays across all of them.

**High fantasy.** Low magic is everywhere and the named threats are political actors. Cantrips are widespread — most villages have a dabbler of some kind; low spells (1st–2nd) are a normal feature of town and city life; only the high end (6th+, guaranteed cures, resurrection) is rare, costly, and plot-tier. Casting basic magic in public is unremarkable. Vaerelloth the Smoke-Wing is a polity you negotiate with, not an encounter you kill in act three. The Silvering elves have a faerie court beneath their visible realm. A vampire holds a borderland duchy under treaty with Aurea. A lich corresponds with the Aurean College of Iron from a buried Aureothan ruin. The dwarves of the White Mountains share their range with Vaerelloth under accord. A sea-thing in the Iron Strait accepts propitiation from raiders who do not name it. The world has wonders, and the wonders have agendas.

**Gritty survival.** The grit lives in the danger, not in magic-scarcity. A basic Cure Wounds from a village hedge-caster is within reach; what stays priced beyond peasants is *reliable, potent* magic — a guaranteed heal at the Eldermouth Codex temple costs more than a fyrdman earns in a year, disease-cure and raise-dead more still. Raise Dead requires Codex Patriarchal authorization, takes a year of fasting from the petitioner, and consumes diamonds the Codex controls; True Resurrection has been performed twice in living memory and both times for kings of Aurea. Meanwhile monsters and Named Threats run high-powered and deadly. Winter kills. Disease kills. The fyrd cannot be levied if the harvest fails. A serious wound left to fester will still kill you, because the reliable high magic that would mend it is out of reach. Adventurer-companies exist but are rare and dangerous to hire — three or four named companies in the whole Marchlands, on retainer to crowns or working for plunder. PCs are precious because most who try the work die.

These three registers are not in tension. They are layered. The feudal politics determine *who* will be sent to deal with the high-fantasy threats; the gritty survival pressure determines *what it costs* to make the journey.

### The Hethrun identity, in two layers

**Norse over Saxon.** Hethrun reads as Danelaw. Norse warriors and trader-rulers conquered an older Saxon population three centuries ago when their own homeland fell, and the layers have only partially fused. The Norse layer shows in the eorls (now functionally jarls), the raiders, the skalds, the Raven cult, the oath-rings, the seafaring orientation, the Iron Strait gaze. The Saxon layer shows in the place-names (*Eldermouth*, *Brackenford*, *Mossdrowen*, *Hollowford* are all Saxon), the fyrd-and-thane structure, the peasant base, the older form of the Sky-Father, the Codex monastery at Hollowford that became syncretic by mixing Saxon-era reverence into Codex liturgy.

**The Codex is the third faith muscling in.** Aurean-imported, organized, hierarchical, increasingly powerful at court. Father Severin of Eldermouth wants the see elevated to a metropolitan; Hollowford monastery sits at the awkward seam where Codex orthodoxy meets older practice. The Burning One is the *fourth* faith, Ostmark's state vengeance-cult, walking into Hethrun in Branwald the Star-Priest's footsteps. Caught between four faiths, most Hethrunfolk pick what they need for what they're doing: Sky-Father for oaths, Raven for the dead, Veiled Mother for childbirth and healing, Codex for the cities and the southern trade.

### What this setting is not

It is not a heroic world. There are no clear-eyed villains and no chosen ones. Every faction thinks itself reasonable. Ranulf of Stonebay genuinely thinks he would serve Hethrun better than the boy Hadric ever could. Branwald the Star-Priest is genuinely charming and patient because he is genuinely waiting for the right wronged person. The elves on the wood-roads are not evil; they are *different*, and slow, and have been watching for a very long time, and may be more frightened than the people who see them. Vaerelloth had a centuries-long plan and someone ruined it and she is angry now — but she is reasonable about it, by her standards.

It is not a forgiving world. PCs die. Resurrection is not a service available for coin. Winter takes the unprepared. A cantrip-patch or a hedge-caster's Cure Wounds buys time, but a deep wound that has turned needs *reliable* healing the village cannot supply, and that will kill the poor over weeks. The named threats are *out of reach* of low-level PCs — Vaerelloth does not fight tier-one parties; she does not need to — and lesser monsters run tough enough to do the killing in the meantime. Adventuring is a vocation chosen by people with nothing else, or people willing to pay the cost.

It is not a generic-fantasy world. Demihumans are rare in the Marchlands and visible only at scale 3 — a half-elven hermit Hagan tolerates, a dwarven smith-quarter that may exist in Eldermouth's harbor district but is small enough that most Hethrunfolk have never been to it, the Greatwood elves who have just emerged for the first time in living memory. Elsewhere in the world demihumans are at scale 5 — peer kingdoms, full statecraft, ambassadors at courts, mixed cities — but here on the human frontier, the Marchlands are humanity's hold.

### Isekai default

PCs arrive in Hethrun as **drift-folk** — people who drift in from other worlds, a known and ongoing phenomenon (the Crossing, ≈8 million New Yorkers torn in at once, is its unprecedented mass-event). They begin with no native equipment for the setting's currency, possibly without shared language, and usually with no locally-useful skills — so the default lot of drift-folk is a dispossessed, exploited, indentured underclass. The hardship is economic, not demonic: most folk don't much care, and a known drift-folk draws a minor stigma or a minor boon by context. Drift-folk are commonly recognized; folk know the type on sight more often than not. The rare drift-folk who keeps class powers is **drift-marked** (see Drift-folk) — a skilled local professional, and worth noticing. A handful of figures will read PCs more deeply than the crowd does: Gythwin the Cunning (immediate, she was expecting someone), the elves on the wood-roads (immediate, they will know what the PCs are within seconds), Master Aldwin (within a week of observation), and Branwald the Star-Priest (he may already know more than the players, and is patient about showing it).

Background features may not transfer cleanly to Hethrun. An Acolyte's "free temple lodging at any temple of your faith" does not function if the PC's faith does not exist here; the GM should improvise an equivalent (Hollowford's syncretism may offer shelter to nearly any plausible faith claim). An Outlander's wilderness lore applies broadly. A Soldier's military rank does not, but the bearing reads. PCs should feel genuinely other-worldly even at low levels; this is a feature.

### Where the magic lives

**Casters in the Marchlands.** Magic is common at the low end and scarce only at the high. Cantrips are widespread — most villages have a dabbler (any class: artificer, wizard, sorcerer, bard, warlock, cleric, or druid). Low spells (1st–2nd) are medium-common: a village will have a hedge-caster, a town several, a city many. Mid magic (3rd–5th) is medium-uncommon: a town runs one or two practitioners, a city several, all for real coin. High magic (6th+), guaranteed cures, and resurrection are the rare, costly, Codex-gated, plot-tier tier. The *named* casters are the notable ones above the hedge-tier — in Hethrun proper: Master Aldwin (court wizard, Eldermouth), Gythwin (cunning-woman, the Heath), Father Severin (Codex divine), Branwald (Burning One pact magic, foreign), Sister Aelfwyn (Codex cleric, Hollowford), plus a traveling alchemist and a half-elven hermit — set among the broad base of village dabblers and town hedge-casters who never make a chronicle. Across the wider Marchlands, every major polity has its own equivalents — court wizards, hedge-witches, monastery-trained clerics — over the same common low-magic base.

**Casters elsewhere in the world.** The *high* register is much higher. The Aurean College of Iron formalizes wizardry; the Codex maintains seminaries that train clerics by the dozen each year; Ostmark's Solar Academy turns out battle-mages who serve in their imperial armies; the Chola Empire's temple-administrations support working clerics in every district; the Silvering and the White Mountains have their own ancient traditions. The Marchlands' low magic is as common as anywhere; what they lack is the concentration of *high* magic the great southern and eastern institutions can field. PCs from elsewhere often find the scarcity of reliable high-tier casting striking, not the absence of magic itself.

**Patrons that exist here.** For warlocks specifically: the Burning One offers Fiend pacts (the Sol Invictus-vengeance face has an infernal-tier aspect older than Ostmark); the Silvering's deep faerie court grants Archfey pacts; the Iron Strait sea-thing grants Great Old One / Fathomless pacts; the lich in the Aureothan ruins grants Undead pacts; Vaerelloth herself grants Draconic pacts (rare and dangerous to negotiate); the Heath holds at least one cursed barrow-blade for Hexblade pacts; an aspect of the Sky-Father's eagle can grant Celestial pacts for those who follow him in the old way; Chola and Sandseas connections can grant Genie pacts. A PC warlock arriving in Hethrun will draw attention proportional to which patron they carry.

### Adventurer status

A mix. The top tier are elite specialist retainers — the **Free Company of the Cloven Sail**, on retainer to the High King at Wessmark, charged with monstrous threats inside the realm; the **Greatwood Wardens**, a folk-heroic operation working the deep Silvering verge with quiet elven approval. The bottom tier are sketchy mercantile-mercenary outfits working out of Stonebay and the Vellanaadu pirate havens — useful, distrusted, knights look down on them. Most PCs end up somewhere in the middle: hired hands for a specific job, with a reputation to build or lose.

### In-world facts that produce the gritty feel

These describe the setting, not the rules. The GM system handles mechanical implementation.

- **Reliable healing magic is expensive at point of cast; basic patching is not.** A village hedge-caster's Cure Wounds is cheap and accessible. What costs dear is *potent, guaranteed* healing: Eldermouth's Codex temple charges roughly a fyrdman's annual wage for a dependable tier-one heal, and more for disease-cure. The Hollowford monastery undercharges quietly because Sister Aelfwyn refuses payment from the poor; the bishop of Eldermouth dislikes this and has not yet moved against her.
- **Resurrection chain is Codex-monopolized and plot-tier.** Raise Dead requires Codex Patriarchal authorization (the Patriarch's seat is in Aurea), a year of fasting from the petitioner, and diamonds the Codex tracks. Revivify is rarely available outside Codex temples; the Hollowford monastery does not stock the components. True Resurrection has been performed twice in living memory; both times the corpse was a king of Aurea.
- **Spell components are scarce in the Marchlands.** Diamonds for Raise Dead come north through Aurea or east from Ostmark, both with their own taxes and politics. Material components for high-tier spells are politically tracked — a wizard buying ten gold pieces of incense in Eldermouth will be noted by someone.
- **The fyrd cannot be levied in winter and is reluctant in spring.** Every fyrdman is needed at home for the planting; pulling them off costs the kingdom in next year's harvest. A bad raid year compounds into a hungry winter that compounds into a weak spring fyrd.
- **Disease is real.** Plague-clocks tick under the surface. Hollowford's monks track outbreaks. A wound left untended will turn; a hedge-caster can ease it, but a true *cure* — a Lesser Restoration or better — is significant wages and most who need one cannot afford one.
- **Permadeath is the default register.** When PCs die they die. The world reflects this: graves matter, oaths to the dead matter, the Raven sees, the Veiled Mother sings the going-out. The GM system handles the mechanics; the lore expects PCs to be precious.

### What the GM should hold across sessions

- Every named faction has a project clock running whether or not the PCs are watching it (see `factions.md` and `active_threats.md`).
- Status with named factions shifts with PC actions; track in the campaign session-log.
- The named threats (Vaerelloth, the lich, the sea-thing, the faerie court, the dragon-cult) are tier-3 and tier-4 problems. They are *there* from session zero; encountering them at low level should feel like being told the wolf is in the woods, not like fighting it.
- The Coronation Clock is short — six weeks at session zero. Most other clocks are months or years. The Coronation is the central crisis the campaign hangs from at low tier; everything else escalates around it.
- The Aureothan past is everywhere if the GM looks for it. Old roads, old coins, old foundations, old laws still half-honored, old gods half-renamed. The current world sits on five hundred years of imperial ruins.

## What the Marchlands are

The Marchlands are six culturally-themed regions on the northern frontier of human dominion, each holding multiple independent polities. They were once the imperial north of Aureoth — the conquering empire that became the great Aureothan civilization, fell five centuries ago in two compounding crises, and never recovered. The provinces fragmented. The roads survived. The Codex Church survived, barely. Everything else is the messy aftermath.

The Marchlands sit between four peer powers: **Aurea** (the French-coded feudal kingdom that emerged in Aureoth's western core) to the south; **Ostmark** (the Byzantine-coded eastern successor state that never broke, holding what was Aureoth's eastern half) across the Iron Strait; **the Silvering** (elven peer realm) deep in the western forests; **the White Mountains** (Tibetan-coded dwarven monastic kingdom, plus Vaerelloth) to the north and northwest. South of the Marchland archipelago lies the **Chola Empire**'s northernmost reach. Further out, the Marchlands hear rumor of the **Sandseas** (Sahel-coded caravan kingdoms south of Aurea), the **Jade Throne** (Chinese-coded vast eastern empire), and the **Sunrise Isles** (Heian-Japan-coded far empire beyond the Jade Throne) — but most Marchlandfolk will never see anyone from any of these in their lives.

## The High Kingship at Wessmark

The High King's seat is at Wessmark, the pure-Saxon central region. The title descends from an Aureothan-era ducal honor that was elevated when the empire fell; the holder is paramount in name across all six regions and de facto ruler of his own duchy and three nominal vassal-duchies. The other five regions acknowledge his title and ignore his commands at need.

**Cyneberht Aelfricsson, High King of the Marchlands**, sits at Wessmark — early sixties, polished, a Saxon man holding an Aureothan-elevated title by ancestral descent from the Wessmark ducal line. He is more capable than his enemies suspect and more constrained than his friends would like. (See `npcs.md` for full sketch.) His three living pressures: a Codex Patriarch in Aurea demanding orthodoxy and threatening the prerogatives of the High Kingship if he does not comply; his own three nominal vassal-duchies playing him against each other while collecting his coin; and the five other Marchland regions ignoring him when convenient — most consistently and politely, Iyendu; most pointedly when it costs him, the offshore Norse jarldoms.

Cyneberht has *quietly* been watching Hethrun's slide toward civil war with grave attention — the more so because Hethrun's late king was his own **Marshal of the High King**, the office through which the planned invasion of Ostmark was being raised, and that mobilization has now turned inward. If the boy Hadric is crowned at the Kingsmound by midwinter, the precedent strengthens the High Kingship's role in legitimizing succession. If Ranulf takes the throne and looks to Ostmark, the High Kingship loses Hethrun's tribute and influence on the northern peninsula entirely; a victory for Edmund or Halfdan would tilt the realm toward Aurea or the deep north instead. Cyneberht will not send the Wessmark host to Hethrun — he cannot afford to and the other duchies would not let him — but he has dispatched a sworn-man as observer.

## The six regions

### 1. Hethrun (Norse-on-Saxon, north peninsula)

The starter region. ~2,000,000 souls in Hethrun — a major Marchland power, not a petty kingdom — the Cloven Strand jarldom offshore a separate Norse polity of perhaps 40,000 more.

**Hethrun kingdom** — the central polity, and a major Marchland power of ~2,000,000 souls. Coastal, fyrd-based, sliding into civil war at session zero. The late king (boy-king Hadric's father) had risen to **Marshal of the High King** and was rallying a Marchlands-wide invasion of the Aureothan Empire (Ostmark, in the east) when he died suddenly — cause ambiguous and suspicious. The mobilized armies and ambitions turned inward, and the realm is now tipping toward civil war, with the Coronation (Hadric must be crowned by midwinter, ~6 weeks out) as the flashpoint. The power-players: the **Regency/Crown** (legitimate but weak); **Eorl Ranulf of Stonebay** (Ostmark-backed, the pretender, already in open war); **Count Edmund** of the Hollowford March (Aurea-backed western hardliner, poised); and **Count Halfdan** (deep-north-backed, moving into the North Coast succession vacuum, poised, his troops already skirmishing Ranulf's raiders). The realm is eight eorldoms (the crown demesne among them) and six independent crown-vassal thanedoms; see `realm_titles.md` for the frame, the eight `eorldom_<name>.md` files and `independent_thanedoms.md` for the detail, and the other Hethrun files.

**The Cloven Strand Jarldom** — offshore islands east of Stonebay, in the Iron Strait. Pure-Norse, independent of Hethrun, recognizes no king. Jarl **Hadolf One-Hand** raids the Aurean coasts and trades stolen goods through Stonebay; he and Eorl Ranulf were sworn brothers in youth and are now wary rivals. The Cloven Strand, with Skarrgaard, is now a patron of **Count Halfdan** in his push into the North Coast succession vacuum. The Cloven Strand has access to an Aureothan-era war engine on one of its islands — a doomsday-weapon Hadolf does not fully understand and refuses to discuss. (See `factions.md`.)

**The Hollowford March** — not a separate polity but one of Hethrun's six independent thanedoms, a crown-vassal county in the southern Greenways held directly of the Hethrun crown. **Count Edmund** of the March is no eorl and sits no Witan, but he is the realm's standing antagonist-county and now one of the civil war's poised power-players — the Aurea-backed western hardliner: he has enclosed the March's common land for two decades, displacing peasants by the hundred, and has corresponded with House Volusen in Aurea throughout. He is excavating an Aureothan ruin on his lands, and what he is finding is changing him. With the war-mobilization turned inward, he is poised to rise. (See `independent_thanedoms.md`.)

### 2. Wessmark (pure-Saxon, High King's seat)

The Saxon heartland that the Norse never overran. ~6,000,000 souls across the High Kingdom proper, three named vassal-duchies, several Codex bishoprics, and scattered Norse Danelaw enclaves on the coast.

**Wessmark Kingdom** — Cyneberht's direct holding. Saxon throughout, fyrd-strong, Sky-Father reverent in its old form (predating Hethrun's Norse-influenced form). Codex Church established but not dominant. The High King's hall sits at **Cynesburh**, the only city in the Marchlands with surviving Aureothan walls intact along their full circuit.

**The Duchy of Reedmarsh** — a vassal-duchy in name. Duke **Wulfgar the Marked** has been suspiciously successful in everything he attempts for the past five years and Cyneberht has begun to wonder why; the Duke has secret backers and the High King's people have not yet identified them. (See `factions.md`.)

**The Codex Bishopric of Aelmsbury** — a Codex see with political teeth. Bishop **Father Aldhelm** sits in the High King's Witan as the Patriarch's representative and pushes Codex interests aggressively. He and Severin of Eldermouth are correspondents.

**Coastal Norse Danelaw enclaves** — small Norse settlements scattered along Wessmark's eastern coast, mostly fishing-and-raiding villages that pay token tribute to the local Saxon thanes and otherwise live as they please. Viking influence runs through Wessmark as it runs through every Marchland region.

### 3. Volotrad (Rus' / Slavic, forest interior)

The forest-interior region. ~3,000,000 souls across the Volotrad grand-principality, two independent river-princedoms, and a Varangian-Norse trade polity that punches above its weight.

**The Volotrad Grand-Principality** — a Slavic grand-principality of log halls, sacred groves, and druzhina warbands sworn personally to **Grand Prince Mstislav of Volotrad**. Mstislav has a concealed sin (the manner of his elder brother's death, twelve years ago) and a hidden magical gift (he sees true names in dreams). The grand-principality holds the central river system and three smaller princedoms recognize it nominally. (See `factions.md`.)

**The River Princedoms** — two independent princely holdings on tributary rivers, each ruled by a cadet of Mstislav's house, each pursuing its own ambitions. The northern one (**Berezgrad**) trades amber north to the White Mountains; the southern one (**Drevograd**) is in covert correspondence with the Codex Patriarch.

**The Varangian Polity of Gardvik** — a Norse trade-warrior colony on a major Volotradan river, paying tribute upriver to Mstislav and de facto ruling its own affairs. Connection to the Hethrun Norse community is real; Hadolf One-Hand has cousins in Gardvik.

### 4. Iyendu (Igbo/Yoruba-coded, forest-edge)

The forest-edge region south-and-inland from Hethrun, through the Greatwood. ~3,800,000 souls across the Iyendu divine-kingdom, two surrounding oba-realms, an Aurean trading factor, and a substantial Mississippian-coded refugee community.

**The Iyendu Kingdom** — ruled by the **Eze Iyendu Obasi-of-the-Bright-Veil**, divine king bound by ancestor-network and elaborate masking-and-festival tradition. The Eze has inherited duties he resents (the divine-king role requires ritual seclusion for three months each year) and is leading a double life through a trusted ally. Smith-caste and diviner-caste are politically powerful. (See `factions.md`.)

**The Two Oba-Realms** — **Mma-Ndu** to the north and **Ile-Akpa** to the south, each ruled by an oba who acknowledges Iyendu's senior position but holds full sovereignty. Mma-Ndu is closer to Iyendu spiritually; Ile-Akpa is closer to the Sandseas economically.

**The Aurean trading factor at Beneside** — a small Aurean enclave with a chartered factor holding monopoly trading rights, attached to the Iyendu kingdom by treaty. Run by **Factor Lucien Avellino**, a House Volusen man and almost certainly a Cassian Drey correspondent.

**The Mississippian-coded refugee community** — some twenty-five thousand people who came south through the Greatwood three generations ago after their own homeland fell to the Tihatchee sun-cult expansion; they hold a designated quarter in Iyendu's capital and are theologically odd (some converted to Iyendu's ancestor-faith; some kept the sun-and-falcon; some made a syncretic peace).

### 5. Tihatchee (Mississippian-coded, river basin)

The river-basin region west of Iyendu, organized around the great Mound at Tihatchee proper. ~2,000,000 souls across the central mound-capital, two tributary chiefdoms, and one chiefdom that has lost its sun-cult orthodoxy and become something else.

**Tihatchee Mound-Capital** — a palisaded chiefdom organized around eight stepped mounds, a sun-and-falcon cult priesthood, copper-and-shell trade extending in all directions, and a falcon-warrior order. The **Suncatcher Eseth-Atohi**, paramount chief and high priest of the Sun-Falcon, rules from the central mound. Tihatchee considers itself the rightful inheritor of an older, broader Mississippian-coded civilization that fragmented before the Aureothan conquest; the refugee community in Iyendu is one piece of the diaspora.

**Two tributary chiefdoms** — **Watontocha** (river-north) and **Kishokota** (river-south), each with its own paramount chief, each acknowledging Tihatchee's spiritual seniority while running its own affairs.

**The Lost-Sun Chiefdom of Mtosi** — the dragon-cult of Vaerelloth. A chiefdom on Tihatchee's western river-edge that abandoned the Sun-Falcon over three generations and now venerates the Smoke-Wing as a sun-replacement. Their **Vessel-Speaker Eda-Hesotka** is in direct communication with Vaerelloth. The cult is the central reason for Vaerelloth's current restlessness. (See `factions.md` for Vaerelloth's polity.)

### 6. Vellanaadu (Chola-coded protectorate, southern archipelago)

The southern archipelago — about forty inhabited islands strung across warmer waters between the Marchlands and the Chola Empire proper. ~1,500,000 souls across the Chola protectorate, native pre-Chola micro-polities, and Norse pirate havens.

**The Vellanaadu Protectorate** — the Chola colony, established eighty years ago by a tributary fleet from the Empire and now a self-governing province. Mercantile-guild armed force, dharmic temple administration, bronze craftworks, dhow-and-temple culture. Governed by **Mandalanaayakan Karthik Vellanaadan**, a Chola-trained governor who reports to the Empire annually and has not been overruled in fifteen years.

**Native pre-Chola archipelagic peoples** — multiple small polities on outer islands, with their own languages and seafaring traditions, some allied to Vellanaadu, some neutral, some hostile. The largest is the **Mowari Confederacy**, twelve outer islands under a confederation of chiefs.

**The Norse Pirate Haven of Skarrgaard** — a Norse pirate-state on three islands at the archipelago's northern edge. Founded by descendants of a raider fleet that was supposed to return to Hethrun a century ago and never did. **Jarl Sigridr Bone-Reader** rules — burnt out from a century of warring with the Chola and tied to a vice (sea-poppy from southern Chola lands) she did not have when she took the seat. Skarrgaard's connection to Hethrun is real; the families remember each other and trade illicit goods through the Iron Strait. Skarrgaard, with the Cloven Strand Jarldom, now backs **Count Halfdan** in the contest for Hethrun's North Coast. (See `factions.md`.)

## Cultural enclaves crossing borders

Borders do not match culture. Examples:

- **The Norse connective tissue.** Norse populations exist in every Marchland region: Hethrun (dominant), the Cloven Strand Jarldom (offshore Hethrun), Wessmark Danelaw coastal enclaves, the Volotradan Varangian polity at Gardvik, Skarrgaard in the Vellanaadu archipelago. Wherever water reaches, Norse have raided, settled, and stayed.
- **The Mississippian diaspora in Iyendu.** Some twenty-five thousand Mississippian-coded refugees in Iyendu's capital quarter, theologically mixed, politically connected to both Iyendu and Tihatchee.
- **A Saxon trader-town in Volotrad.** **Eldenhof**, a Saxon settlement on a Volotradan trade-road, founded by Wessmark merchants two centuries ago, now bilingual and culturally hybrid.
- **A Chola merchant quarter in Stonebay.** Forty households of Chola traders who came north for amber and stayed, now generations established. They worship a Chola river-god under a small temple-roof and pay token Codex respect in public. Branwald the Star-Priest has visited their temple twice; they distrust him.
- **The Aurean factor at Beneside.** A small Aurean enclave inside the Iyendu kingdom, full of House Volusen intelligence personnel and a chartered trade monopoly.
- **The Codex Church.** Not a cultural enclave but a transnational institution; Codex bishoprics exist in every region, including Iyendu and Volotrad where the Codex is decidedly a minority faith working hard to expand.

## Inter-region politics at session zero

- **The High King watches Hethrun.** Cyneberht has an observer in Eldermouth (see `npcs.md`) and will not intervene militarily but will use influence where possible.
- **Aurea presses south on Hethrun.** Cassian Drey's tribute demand is part of a wider Aurean pattern: House Volusen wants Marchland holdings as marriage-and-debt assets. Volusen's factor at Beneside in Iyendu does similar work.
- **Ostmark presses east on Hethrun and Wessmark Danelaw.** Branwald in Stonebay is part of a wider Ostmark pattern: the Burning One's vengeance-cult is recruiting wronged Marchlanders. Cyneberht knows this and has not yet decided how to respond.
- **The Tihatchee dragon-cult presses west on Wessmark and north on Iyendu.** No Marchland court yet sees the dragon-cult clearly; it does not announce itself. The Suncatcher of Tihatchee knows but is constrained by ritual obligation and his own court politics.
- **The Chola protectorate manages Norse piracy in Vellanaadu** — a slow, expensive, unending war. The Empire has not yet decided whether to commit more force to wipe out Skarrgaard or to negotiate. This is a clock.
- **Volotrad sells amber north** to the White Mountain dwarves and watches its southern flank for Ostmark pressure. Grand Prince Mstislav's hidden magical gift has begun to show him things about the dwarves he wishes he could un-see.

## How the High Kingship actually works

- **Title.** Paramount in name across all six regions; descended from an Aureothan-era ducal honor.
- **De facto rule.** Cyneberht's direct holdings: the Wessmark Kingdom (his duchy proper). Nominal vassals: three Wessmark duchies (Reedmarsh, plus two others not detailed here) and the Codex Bishopric of Aelmsbury.
- **Powers regularly exercised.** Calls the Marchland Witan (a council of regional representatives that meets when summoned; last met four years ago). Mints High King's coin (used everywhere; competing with Aurean and Ostmark coin in border regions). Endorses successions (a coronation at the Kingsmound is legitimized by High King's writ; without it the boy Hadric's claim is shaky).
- **Powers occasionally exercised.** Adjudicates inter-region disputes (rare; most disputes settle without him). Declares Marchland-wide war (last done eighty years ago against a Sandseas raider fleet that was never seen again).
- **Powers asserted but rarely enforced.** Levies men from the regions for the Wessmark host (Iyendu has never sent men; Volotrad sent men once and refused again; the Cloven Strand laughed at the messenger).
- **What the High Kingship is, in practice.** A coordinating fiction that keeps the Marchland regions from outright war with each other most of the time, supplies a legitimating writ to coronations that need one, and serves as a partial check on Aurea and Ostmark's southern and eastern pressure. Cyneberht plays the role exactly to its constraints and rarely tries to expand it.
