# The Independent Thanedoms of Hethrun

The six crown-vassal counties of Hethrun — thanelands held of the Crown directly, under no eorl. This file covers all six together: the Hollowford March, Saltwick, Wardmoor, Eldfield, Hartshaw, and Stormness. The realm frame, the title hierarchy, and the eorldom summaries are in `realm_titles.md`; the eorldoms themselves are each in their own `eorldom_<name>.md`.

## The independent thanedoms — essence

An independent thanedom is a thaneland held of the Crown of Hethrun directly, under no eorl. Their thanes are count-rank — the same rank as a hold within an eorldom — but they answer only to the King, which makes them free agents in a way no eorl's vassal can be. They do not sit the Witan, and so cast no vote in the Coronation; but they hold fyrd-spears, they hold land athwart roads and coasts the realm needs, and their public backing is read by the Witan as a weathervane. In the Coronation crisis the six are courted directly by both sides, with no eorl mediating — which makes them, between them, a small loose bloc that either faction would be glad to claim. They are scattered: a southern march, two coastal counties, a Heath-frontier watch, a court-shadowed farm-county, and a forest-edge county half-given to the Greatwood.

## The Hollowford March — the county

The Hollowford March is a crown-thanedom of the southern Greenways, bordering the Wealdvale eorldom, and it is the realm's standing antagonist-county. Count Edmund holds it, and Count Edmund is an encloser: across two decades he has been fencing the March's common land for his own demesne — for sheep, for cash-crops, for the Aurean trade — and turning the freemen who farmed it off the land. The displaced have fled north into the crown demesne, west into the Wealdvale, and south across the Aurean border, and the trail of ruined villages they leave behind is the most visible injustice in Hethrun. The March is, for Edmund, a success: it is richer, tidier, and more productive than it was. For everyone downstream of it, it is a wound that will not close. Edmund keeps the March hostile to outsiders — his retainers post checkpoints on the southern road and question every traveller — because a county run this way does not love witnesses.

## The Hollowford March — Count Edmund and his hall

**Seat & character.** Count Edmund holds the March from Hollowham, a hard, controlled, prosperous county-town — community tags *Trade Hub · Oppressed* — where the Aurean southern trade is handled and the count's writ is absolute.
**Count Edmund** — a cold, legal-minded, modernising man of fifty-five who regards the enclosures as improvement and the ruined peasants as an acceptable and frankly inevitable cost. He is not cruel for its own sake; he is something colder, a man who has stopped seeing the people his policy destroys.
**Wants:** the March richer and tidier still; the enclosures completed; the Aurean markets secured; no eorl, ever, set over him.
**Secret:** Edmund's enclosure programme runs on Aurean money. He is in standing correspondence with House Volusen, whose coin funds the improvements and whose merchants take the March's wool and cash-crops — and that correspondence has quietly made Edmund an instrument of Volusen's wider play in Hethrun. (House Sedgemoor of Brackenford and House Faldon of the Wealdvale are tangled in the same web; see `eorldom_brackenford.md` and `eorldom_wealdvale.md`.)
**The excavation.** A separate, quieter matter: Edmund has uncovered an Aureothan ruin on the March's lands and is excavating it, cautiously, toward a sealed inner chamber. Something in it is already changing him — and his Aurean Patriarchate correspondents are urging him to open it faster. This is tracked as Clock 8 in `active_threats.md`; fully opened, the chamber precipitates a regional crisis.
**Coronation lean:** self-interested above all — but the Volusen money currently tilts Edmund toward the regency, the way it tilts Sedgemoor; his loyalty is to the March's expansion and to nothing else.
**Hook:** the Hollowford March is the realm's antagonist-county, and Edmund is a villain a party can oppose without ambiguity — but he is a *legal* villain, doing nothing the crown has forbidden, and bringing him down means evidence, courts, and the right eorl's ear, not a sword.

## The Hollowford March — the enclosure lands

The March's lands are rich southern-Greenways farmland, increasingly fenced into great demesne-blocks, and the country between the fences tells the story. **Three abandoned villages** stand within the March — Ashleigh, Wraxby, and Crows-Marsh — emptied by the enclosures over the last two years, their populations "moved away for better prospects" in the count's phrasing and displaced by force in plain fact. The villages stand mostly intact, slowly dismantled for materials, squatted here and there by the desperate. They are small empty dungeon-sites; they are also political evidence — proof, mapped and dated, of Edmund's enclosure pattern, worth a great deal in the right court (the Wealdvale's hall-moot, the regency, a faction looking to discredit a Volusen client). The southern road runs checkpointed through the March, Edmund's retainers suspicious of every comer. (Bestiary: the Road-Outlaws, `bestiary.md` Group 4A; displaced refugees are a constant non-combat encounter.)

## Hollowford Monastery — the house

Within the Hollowford March, but emphatically not of it, stands Hollowford Monastery — a Codex house in the Greenways, four days south of Eldermouth, three days east of Brackenford, some three hundred souls of monks, lay-brothers, and novices, with a village of roughly two hundred grown up around it under Count Edmund's nominal authority. Two facts define it.

**Heavy Fortification.** The monastery is walled far beyond what the quiet Greenways warrant — stone walls fifteen feet high, watchtowers at the four corners, a standing garrison of twenty lay-brothers trained in arms. The walls were extended four centuries ago by a third abbot who feared a Wessmark incursion that never came, and have been maintained ever since — partly as a discipline, and partly because there is, in fact, something the monastery is fortified *around* (see below).

**Miserable Penury.** Hollowford is the poorest Codex house in the Marchlands. The Patriarchate denies it subsidies and donor-connections because the abbess will not enforce the prohibition on cooperation with the Old Faith — the monastery's hospital treats the poor for free, Old Faith folk and cunning-women among them — and the slow starvation is the Patriarchate's punishment for it. The silvers were sold seven years ago; the altar is wood, the candles tallow; the house subsists on its own garden and the local peasants' donations and is perpetually in debt for parchment, wax, and altar-wine.

## Hollowford Monastery — Sister Aelfwyn and the vault

**Sister Aelfwyn**, Abbess of Hollowford, is sixty-eight, frail in body and iron in will, and has held the line against the Patriarchate for fifteen years at the cost of her house's prosperity. She is one of the genuinely good powers in the realm, and she is estranged from Count Edmund — she has not spoken to him in two years, since the enclosures began emptying her congregation. Her prior, **Brother Oslac of the Vault**, fifty-four, is the only other soul permitted into the chapel's locked vault, an arrangement made when Aelfwyn's health began to fail.

**The vault** beneath the chapel is the heart of the house's mystery. Aelfwyn keeps its only key; the senior monks have not entered it in eighty years; by tradition it holds "what must be guarded," and what that is passes only from abbess to successor. It is the reason for the walls. **The novice Cynwise**, sixteen, has lately begun a series of vivid dreams — the Heath, the barrows, a chant she does not know but can sing — and Aelfwyn is afraid, because she knows what the dreams mean and what they may have to do with the vault (the dream-clock is tracked in `active_threats.md`). Two other threads run through Hollowford: the Codex relic-courier who vanished some weeks ago was last seen passing the monastery without stopping — unusual, and noted — and Aelfwyn has sent a discreet inquiry of her own. **Hooks:** Aelfwyn will welcome a trustworthy party's help with Cynwise's dreams, with the missing courier, and — only once deeply trusted, as a long-arc matter — with the vault itself; her trained lay-brothers, never hired out, are sometimes paired with parties she trusts.

## Saltwick — the crown's eastern port

Saltwick is a crown-thanedom on the Iron Strait coast, a mercantile port on the coast road between Stonebay and Eldermouth. It exists, in the crown's quiet design, as a counterweight: a harbour the Crown holds directly so that Stonebay — and so Eorl Ranulf — does not hold a monopoly on the realm's eastern trade. Saltwick is a smaller, hungrier port than Stonebay, and it lives on exactly the trade Stonebay's guild-monopoly squeezes out, the grey-market and the unchartered cargo included; its seat-town carries the community tags *Trade Hub · Sealed Lips*.

**Count Osmaer** is a shrewd, affable, calculating merchant-count of fifty whose loyalty runs precisely as deep as Saltwick's interest. He knows what Saltwick is for, and he knows that the Coronation crisis has made his little port valuable: Ranulf would dearly like to absorb the rival harbour, on whatever terms; the regency needs Saltwick to stay free and crown-held as the check on Stonebay it was always meant to be.
**Coronation lean:** for sale, honestly and openly — Osmaer will back the side that best secures Saltwick's trade and independence, and both factions can bid. Staying free favours the regency, and Osmaer knows it, which sets his price.
**Hook:** Saltwick is a county-sized prize in open contention — a party can be hired to court Osmaer, to blackmail or bribe him, to protect Saltwick from a Stonebay squeeze, or to dig into the smuggling that funds his independence.

## Wardmoor — the Heath-frontier watch

Wardmoor is a crown-thanedom on the Harrowmark's southern border — an old soldier's watch-thanedom warding the frontier where the Heath's moor meets the lowland. It is a hard, plain, modestly poor county whose reason for being is the watch: its burh, also called Wardmoor, carries the community tags *Border Town · Honest Folk*, a town of soldiers' families that has held its post for generations.

**Count Wulfhere** is an old fyrd-captain raised to a count's seat — dutiful, blunt, contemptuous of court intrigue, a man who keeps Wardmoor the way a good captain keeps a post. He takes the Heath seriously, and the barrow-stirrings beyond his border have begun to worry him in earnest.
**Coronation lean:** regency, for lawful order — a stable realm keeps the frontier funded and the chain of command clear, and Wulfhere has no patience for anything that weakens either. He and Eorl Hereward of the Harrowmark are natural allies, two frontier men holding a danger the lowland will not fund.
**Hook:** Wardmoor is a reliable ally and a steady patron on the Heath frontier; Wulfhere's growing alarm that the barrow-folk's stirrings are reaching over his border is an early, sober warning of the Harrowmark's central threat (see `eorldom_harrowmark.md` and `active_threats.md`), offered by a man not given to alarm.

## Eldfield — the courtier's county

Eldfield is a crown-thanedom of the Greenways close by Eldermouth — rich, gentle farmland, one of the most comfortable counties in Hethrun, and the one whose count is least often in it. Its seat is a prosperous, decent farm-town (*Trade Hub · Honest Folk*) that effectively runs itself, because **Count Leofwine** lives at the royal court.

Leofwine is a smooth, ambitious, deeply embedded courtier-count of forty-eight, a fixture of the regency's Eldermouth circles, and Eldfield's wealth exists, as he sees it, to fund his presence and his manoeuvres there. His true arena is the royal court, not his county.
**Coronation lean:** regency, publicly and — for now — really, because the regency is where Leofwine's ambition is invested. But his deeper loyalty is to his own advancement: he is angling to have his line raised, by a great office of the regency, a royal wardship, or a marriage near the crown, and his backing will last exactly as long as the regency keeps feeding that ambition.
**Hook:** Eldfield is a window into royal-court intrigue — Leofwine knows the regency's currents better than almost anyone outside the Royal Hall — and Leofwine's ambition is itself a lever: secure his next step for him, or threaten it, and the count is moved. A party can also find Eldfield's absentee county a quietly useful, lightly-watched base.

## Hartshaw — the forest-edge county

Hartshaw is a crown-thanedom on the Greatwood verge near Mossdrowen — a forested county half-given over to the wood, the thanedom most entangled with the Greatwood and least interested in the lowland's throne. Its seat is a quiet forest-edge town (*Tradition-Bound · Hidden Lore*), Old Faith to the bone, where the wood's nearness is felt in every custom.

**Count Cynehelm** is a woodsman more than a politician — a calm, deep-rooted, unhurried man of sixty whose family has lived alongside the Greatwood longer and more intimately than any other in Hethrun. Hartshaw keeps old truces of passage with the wood-road elves, observances older than the Codex, and a way of thinking that runs by the wood's rules rather than the court's. To Cynehelm the Coronation crisis is a small, loud, lowland quarrel, and he means Hartshaw to have no part in it.
**Coronation lean:** disengaged — genuinely, not coyly; Cynehelm will not be drawn, and a faction's effort to recruit Hartshaw is most likely to end in courteous, total failure.
**Hook:** Hartshaw is a second door into the Greatwood and the elf-threads, alongside Mossdrowen — Cynehelm's old truces and the county's wood-lore are a resource for a party working toward the elves (see `eorldom_mossdrowen.md`), and the count's flat disengagement is itself a kind of hook: the one county no faction can move, and the puzzle of why.

## Stormness — the storm-coast county

Stormness is a crown-thanedom on the North Coast approaches — a windswept storm-coast headland county, raider-troubled like the eorldom it borders, generations ago carved out of the North Coast eorldom and made a separate crown-thanedom. Its seat is a hard, hammered coastal town (*Border Town · Battle Site*), no stranger to the raiders' fires.

**Count Beorhtsige** is a watchful, weather-hard headland count of fifty-two, beleaguered by the same unidentified raiders harrying the North Coast, and consumed by the one question that bears directly on his county's survival: who follows the dying Eorl Berthwald. A North Coast eorl of the wrong temper might try to reabsorb Stormness into the eorldom it was cut from, and Beorhtsige means to prevent that.
**Coronation lean:** uncommitted on the throne itself — but very far from neutral on the North Coast succession, where he backs whichever of Berthwald's rival heirs is least likely to threaten Stormness's separate standing (in practice the lawful heir, Wulfric the Younger, which tilts Beorhtsige faintly regency-ward by consequence).
**Hook:** Stormness is a player in the Berthwald succession (see `eorldom_northcoast.md`) and a front-line county in the coast's raider trouble — a party can be hired against the raiders, drawn into Beorhtsige's manoeuvring over the North Coast eorldom, or find in Stormness a second vantage on the sea-thing threads of the western coast.

## At a glance — the thanedoms in the crisis

The six independent thanedoms cast no Witan vote, but their fyrd and their public backing are a loose bloc both factions want. They divide cleanly. **Regency-leaning:** Wardmoor (for frontier order) and Eldfield (a courtier's investment), with Stormness tilting the same way by consequence of the North Coast succession. **In open contention:** Saltwick, honestly for sale, with the regency's interest in keeping it free of Stonebay set against Ranulf's hunger to absorb it. **Self-directed or out of play:** the Hollowford March, serving only Count Edmund's enclosures and the Volusen money behind them; and Hartshaw, genuinely disengaged. The thanedoms' real campaign value is as doors: the Hollowford March into the enclosure crisis and the Wealdvale's central quarrel, Hollowford Monastery into the barrow-dream and missing-courier threads, Wardmoor and Stormness into the Heath and sea-coast dangers, Hartshaw into the Greatwood, and Eldfield into the royal court itself. For the realm frame and the cross-eorldom web see `realm_titles.md`; for the crisis clocks see `active_threats.md`.
