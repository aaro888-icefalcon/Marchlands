# Court of Hethrun

The realm's central government: the **royal court at Eldermouth** (the boy-king's household and the regency around Isolde) and the **Witan of Hethrun** (the council of eorls and chief officers). Both meet in the same hall and share people, but they function differently and the GM holds them as separate forces. The city of Eldermouth itself, the crown demesne, and its crown-thanes are in `eorldom_eldermouth.md`; this file is the court and the council. The Coronation Clock is in `active_threats.md`; the realm-wide vote board is in `realm_titles.md`.

## The royal court at Eldermouth — essence

The royal court is the household of the boy-king Hadric: twelve years old, eight months on the throne, in a regency administered by his mother Lady Isolde of Aurea, with Master Aldwin and Sigevard the Silver-Tongue in advisory roles. It sits in Eldermouth's royal hall and outbuildings, some two hundred residents counting servants, retainers, and the boy-king's companions. Court is a daily gathering at the king's hearth; formal Witan sessions are infrequent and separately convened.

## The royal court — Decadent Court

Since Aelfric the Just's death, and sharply in the past three months, the royal court has drifted into indulgences improper to a regency in crisis. The causes: Lady Isolde's Aurean-trained preference for elaborate court life; Aurean envoy Cassian Drey encouraging it deliberately for his own ends; and grief and uncertainty pushing the older retainers toward escape rather than the boy-king's training.

Manifestations at court: Aurean wine and luxury imports at the queen-mother's request, paid from a depleting treasury; an expanded musical establishment, three musicians hired in two months; long evening feasts displacing the old Hethrun custom of early dinners, so Hadric sleeps too late and his tutors complain; Cassian Drey's compound treated as a court extension, where Isolde and the senior ladies dine twice weekly; and diminished oversight of the boy's training — Aldwin has not given Hadric a lesson in three weeks, Sigevard has twice been turned away by Isolde's people, and the Sky-Father priest meant to teach him oath-craft is fobbed off to inconvenient hours.

## The royal court — New Generation

The court's prior leadership has been gutted. Aelfric the Just is eight months dead; Crown Prince Wystan three years; two senior thanes, Aelfric's right hands, died within a year of the king; the old court wizard died six years ago and Aldwin took the role only nominally before retreating from court politics. The court is now staffed by people too young or too new: Isolde is thirty-two; her chief steward, Brother Eadgar, is twenty-seven and promoted last spring; the boy-king's chief tutor is new to court life.

Manifestations at court: inexperienced decision-making — Isolde's instincts are Aurean, not Hethrun, and she misreads situations her mother's court would have read correctly; bold actions beyond her standing — her correspondence with House Volusen began six months ago and overreaches, her steward has approved expenditures the treasury cannot bear, and Hadric received a Codex blessing at Cassian Drey's chapel four weeks ago that an older retainer would have prevented; and a leadership vacuum that someone will fill — Ranulf builds toward it from Stonebay, Cuthbert positions, Branwald and Cassian Drey both wait, and the court's elders are half-withdrawn.

## The royal court — active dynamics

Four currents run under the court. **The regency against Hadric's true interests** — Isolde's choices (the Aurean rapprochement, the lost training time) do not serve the boy's long-term position; Aldwin and Sigevard see it, Hadric does not. **The Codex push** — Severin wants Hadric publicly Codex-blessed for the diocese's advantage, and Cassian Drey hosted exactly that at his compound with no Sky-Father priest present; Old Faith resentment is real and growing. **Ranulf's reach into the court** — Cedric of Stonebay is Hadric's companion, a laundry-mistress in Isolde's household has a brother in the Stonebay fyrd, and the Bone-Sayers run at least three more unidentified relays into Eldermouth. **Hadric's dragon-blood awakening** — minor signs now; within two weeks he will heat his bathwater unintentionally, within two months speak draconic syllables asleep (see `active_threats.md`); Aldwin will notice early, and so might the wood-road elves watching Eldermouth from the western hills.

## The Witan of Hethrun — essence and powers

The Witan is the council of the kingdom's senior figures — the seven eorls, the chief Codex bishop, the court wizard, the court skald, the queen-mother, and certain other notables — convened to deliberate matters requiring their consensus. Coronations require Witan endorsement; wars require its call; major treaties its ratification. Day-to-day governance does not need it. The Witan is currently in extended deliberation over the Coronation, sitting every two weeks, with no decision reached in three months. The six independent thane-counts (see `realm_titles.md`) do not sit the Witan, though their backing is courted outside it.

## The Witan — Affliction's Mark

The Witan as a body is gnawed by a persistent illness no member would admit to: a deepening paralysis whose true name is *not knowing how to choose*. Some is genuine indecision — the succession is genuinely fraught. Some is corrupted decision-making — Cassian Drey's pressure, Branwald's quiet influence, Ranulf's Bone-Sayer information operations. Some is something else, a quasi-magical condition Aldwin has noted privately and not named.

Aldwin's hypothesis: the elves, when they come down from the wood-roads, carry a faint *something* that makes those near them more uncertain, more cautious, more willing to defer. The Witan has met three weeks running in chambers within a hundred miles of where the wood-roads have appeared. Aldwin has told no one — if he is right, the elves have, by design or not, been delaying the Coronation. Manifestations: three consecutive deadlocked sessions; eorls voting against their own factions at odd moments, Cuthbert especially erratic; a low-grade headache several members report without connecting; and a deepening court-wide fatigue — Aldwin sleeps poorly, Sigevard's verse has lost its bite, Isolde is more anxious than her position warrants.

## The Witan — Sublime Skill

Despite its paralysis, the Witan is extraordinarily good at the work its society most values in such a council: negotiating consensus across factions that should not naturally cohere. This is the Hethrun political tradition at its best — the Witan emerged from the original Norse-Saxon fusion three centuries ago specifically to hold both populations together, and its consensus-craft has been refined ever since. Sigevard is at the top of his form in it; Aldwin has been a respected facilitator for thirty years; even Severin, a Codex outsider, has learned to operate within it. The Witan's failure to decide the Coronation is not from lack of skill — it is because the skill is being deployed at full force in three directions at once.

Manifestations: an elaborate ceremonial protocol performed even in extended sessions, every voice heard and every concern formally noted; an effective conflict-prevention mechanism — no physical violence in the chamber in seventy years, the protocols holding under stress; a high standard for compromise-formulation, so that a Witan-blessed settlement, when it comes, carries greater legitimacy than any king-imposed one; and the skald-mediation tradition — Sigevard murmurs the proceedings to a memorial-clerk, and skaldic memory is binding, so decisions made under his witness cannot later be denied.

## The Coronation deadlock

The deadlock has three components.

**Ranulf's open contest.** Ranulf has not formally claimed the throne, nor committed to support Hadric. He makes the correct procedural argument — a boy-king cannot be properly crowned without Witan consensus — while himself preventing that consensus. He is backed in non-voting capacity by Branwald and, privately, by Hadolf One-Hand of Cloven Strand, both of whom prefer no Aurean-leaning regency on their flank; he is opposed by Severin and the queen-mother. Among the eorls, only Ketil leans his way, softly, and Hagan drifts toward whoever last had his ear.

**The Witan's procedural conscience.** The Hethrun tradition will not crown a king without consensus. Forcing Hadric's coronation over Ranulf's resistance would damage the realm's legitimacy in ways the older Witan members — Aldwin, Sigevard, Aelfwin, even Severin — will not accept, though several privately favour Hadric.

**The unidentified delay-pressure.** Aldwin's elven-influence hypothesis, plus Cassian Drey's slow-tribute negotiation and Branwald's patience. Each wants delay for its own reasons.

The Coronation must occur by midwinter — the forty-second day after Hadric enters his thirteenth year, by old Hethrun tradition — or his claim is procedurally compromised. The Coronation Clock is one of the campaign's central clocks; see `active_threats.md`.

## What can break the deadlock

- **Ranulf openly claims the throne.** The cleanest break; he waits for the Witan to ask him, and the Witan will not.
- **Cassian Drey forces a decision.** If Aurea makes the tribute payment depend on a settled succession, the regency must act fast.
- **Branwald's working completes.** If Branwald moves from observation to action, Ranulf's position consolidates rapidly under Ostmark cover.
- **Hadric's dragon-blood manifests publicly.** A clear sign of heroic greatness would push consensus toward him irreversibly; Aldwin and Severin would each handle a public manifestation very differently.
- **An external crisis.** A raid, a war, a fire — an outside force compelling an emergency decision. Several campaign hooks lead here, and the PCs may be the agents of it.
- **A Witan member dies or is incapacitated.** This shifts the consensus immediately, in either direction.
- **A swing eorl is won.** Securing Hereward, Osgar, or Ketil — each winnable on known terms — shifts the board without anyone dying.

## The High King's observer

Cyneberht's observer is resident at Eldermouth: a Wessmark thane, **Wulfgar of Cynesburh** (no relation to Duke Wulfgar the Marked of Reedmarsh — the name is common in Wessmark). He attends court openly as the High King's witness, attends the Witan as a non-voting observer, and writes to Cyneberht weekly. The Witan tolerates him; Severin welcomes him as a Codex-friendly counterweight to Ranulf; Ranulf treats him politely and reveals nothing.

Wulfgar of Cynesburh is a competent middle-aged Saxon thane, here to report, not to influence. But if the Witan asks the High King for arbitration, Wulfgar is the conduit, and his report shaping Cyneberht's position would be decisive — one of the channels by which the Coronation Clock could resolve.
